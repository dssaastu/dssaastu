4.3 Understanding State

Brainstorming:

4.3.1 Defining State

The term 'state' has been used to refer to a bewildering range of things: a collection of institutions, a territorial unit, a philosophical idea, an instrument of coercion or oppression, and so on. This confusion stems, in part, from the fact that the state has been understood in four quite different ways; from an idealist perspective, a functionalist perspective, an organizational perspective and an international perspective.

The idealist approach to the state is most clearly reflected in the writings of Hegel.

Defining State: Idealist Approach (Hegel)

Hegel identified three moments of social existence: the family, civil society and the state.

  • Family: Encourages 'particular altruism' for own children/relatives.
  • Civil Society: Seen as sphere of 'universal egoism' where individuals place own interests before others.
  • State: Conceived as ethical community underpinned by mutual sympathy – 'universal altruism'.

Drawback of idealism: fosters uncritical reverence for the state; fails to distinguish clearly between institutions that are part of the state and those outside it.

Defining State: Functionalist & Organizational

Functionalist approaches:

Focus on role/purpose of state institutions. Central function: maintenance of social order. State defined as set of institutions upholding order and delivering stability (adopted by Neo-Marxists). Weakness: tends to associate *any* institution maintaining order (family, mass media, unions, church) with the state itself.

The organizational view:

Defines state as apparatus of government in broadest sense; set of institutions recognizably 'public' (responsible for collective organization, funded publicly). Virtue: distinguishes clearly between state (bureaucracy, military, police, courts, social security system - the 'body politic') and civil society. Allows talking about 'rolling forward' or 'rolling back' the state (expanding/contracting responsibilities).

Defining State: International Approach & Features

The international approach:

Views state primarily as actor on world stage; basic 'unit' of international politics. Highlights dualistic structure: inward-looking face (relations with own society) and outward-looking face (relations with other states).

Classic definition found in Montevideo Convention on the Rights and Duties of the State (1933). Article 1: state has four features:

  1. A defined territory
  2. A permanent population
  3. An effective government
  4. Sovereignty

Features of State: Population

Population: First essential element. How much population? No exact number. Wide variance (China/India > 1 billion; Vatican/San Marino < few thousand).

Should population be homogeneous? Determined by common factors (religion, blood, language, culture)? Good for integration, but not essential. Most states heterogeneous; nation building marked by diversity. Problems of nation building solved irrespective of differences.

Signifies 'unity in diversity'. Noted that without population there can be no state, 'it goes without saying that an uninhabited portion of the earth, cannot form a state.'

Features of State: Defined Territory

No state without territory. Includes land, water, airspace; maritime jurisdiction up to 3 miles (some claim 12-20 miles). Authority extends to ships on high seas under its flag, embassies/legations abroad.

Size not fixed factor. Large states (China, Russia) and small (Fiji, Mauritius). Also possible island states (Indonesia, Philippines, Japan).

Boundary lines must be well marked out. Can be done geographically (seas, rivers, mountains, forests) or artificially (digging trenches, fixing posts/wire fencing).

Features of State: Government

Said to be soul of the state. Implements state's will. Protects against insecurity. If state regarded as first condition of civilized life, government maintains law/order, makes 'good life' possible. Machinery terminating anarchy.

Universally recognized need for mechanism to bring about/maintain workable arrangement to keep people together. Government should be organized to maintain peace/security.

Form may be monarchical, aristocratic, oligarchic, democratic, dictatorial. What really needed is that if no government, there is anarchy and state is at an end.

Features of State: Sovereignty

Fourth essential attribute. Highest power distinguishing state from other associations. Simplest sense: principle of absolute/unlimited power.

Two aspects:

  • Internal Sovereignty: Implies state power final source of all laws internally. No other authority may claim equality.
  • External Sovereignty: Implies state should be free from foreign control. State willingly accepts international obligations in membership (e.g., UN).

Existence of sovereign authority appears in law. Law binding on all. Universally accepted sovereign state legally competent to issue any command binding citizens/associations.

Features of State: Recognition (Contemporary)

In addition to 1933 features, contemporary theorists and UN consider recognition as fifth essential attribute. For political unit to be accepted as state with 'international personality', must be recognized as such by significant portion of international stage (other states, international/non-governmental organizations).

Recognition implies both approaching necessary facts and desire to come into effect of legal/political results. Likewise, for government to be formally recognized as legitimate government, must be recognized by other governments.

4.4 Rival Theories of State

Brainstorming:

What is the nature of state power, and whose interests does the state represent?

Various rival theories offer different accounts of origins, development, impact. Intense controversy about nature of state power; goes to heart of ideological/theoretical disagreements.

Relate to questions like: Is state autonomous/independent? Essentially product of society? Reflection of broader distribution of power? Does state serve common/collective good, or biased favoring privileged groups? Similarly, is state positive/constructive force, or negative/destructive?

Andrew Heywood (2013) classified rival theories into four: pluralist, capitalist, leviathan, patriarchal.

4.4.1 The Pluralist State

Clear liberal lineage. Stems from belief state acts as 'umpire' or 'referee' in society. View dominated mainstream political analysis, especially Anglo-American thought. Uncommon in tradition for state to be dismissed as abstraction; courts, civil service, military seen as independent actors.

Approach possible based on underlying, often unacknowledged, assumptions about state neutrality. State seen as impartial arbiter or referee that can be bent to will of government.

Origins traced to social-contract theories (Hobbes, Locke). Principal concern: grounds of political obligation, grounds individual obliged to obey/respect state. Argued state arisen out of voluntary agreement (social contract) by individuals recognizing only establishment of sovereign power could safeguard them from insecurity/disorder/brutality of 'state of nature'.

As Locke put it, where there is no law there is no freedom.

The Pluralist State (Continued)

Liberal theory sees state as neutral arbiter among competing groups/individuals; 'umpire' protecting each citizen from encroachments of others. Neutrality reflects fact state acts in interests of all citizens, therefore represents common good/public interest.

Hobbes' view: stability/order secured only through absolute/unlimited state, power not challenged. Locke: developed more typical liberal defense of limited state. State specific: restricted to defense of 'natural' rights (life, liberty, property). Clear distinction between state responsibilities (domestic order, property enforcement) and individual responsibilities (realm of civil society). Locke believed citizens enjoy protection against state, which could be delivered via constitutional/representative government.

Ideas developed into pluralist theory of state in 20th century. Pluralism asserts power widely/evenly dispersed. State neutral, insusceptible to influence of particular groups/interests. Not biased, doesn't have own interests. State 'servant of society'. As Schwarzmantel (1994) put it, state 'the state is the servant of society and not its master'.

The Pluralist State (Neo-Pluralism)

State can be portrayed as 'pincushion' passively absorbing pressures exerted upon it. Two key assumptions:

  1. State effectively subordinate to government. Non-elected bodies (civil service, judiciary, police, military) strictly impartial, subject to authority of political masters. State apparatus therefore thought to conform to principles of public service/political accountability.
  2. Democratic process meaningful/effective. Party competition/interest-group activity ensure government sensitive/responsive to public opinion. State like 'weather vane' blown whichever direction public-at-large dictates.

Modern pluralists adopted more critical view: neo-pluralist theory (Dahl, Lindblom). Acknowledge modern industrialized states more complex/less responsive than classical pluralism suggested. Neo-pluralists, for instance, acknowledge business enjoys 'privileged position' influencing government.

Politics and Markets (Lindblom, 1980): major investor/employer in society, business bound to exercise sway over any government, whatever its ideological leanings. Neo-pluralists accept state *can* forge own sectional interests. This elite (senior civil servants, judges, police chiefs, military leaders) may pursue bureaucratic interests (state growth) or interests of client groups. State regarded as political actor in own right, viewed as powerful interest group. Encouraged Eric Nordlinger (1981) to develop state-centered model based on 'the autonomy of the democratic state'.

4.4.2 The Capitalist State

Marxist notion offers clear alternative to pluralist image of state as neutral arbiter/umpire. Marxists argue state cannot be understood separately from economic structure of society. View usually understood in terms of classic formulation: state nothing but instrument of class oppression; state emerges out of, and reflects, class system.

Rich debate within Marxism moved theory beyond this classic formulation. Scope to revise Marxist attitudes stems from ambiguities in Marx's own writings.

Marx did not develop systematic theory. General sense: state part of 'superstructure' determined/conditioned by economic 'base' (real foundation of social life). Precise relationship unclear. Two theories identifiable in Marx's writings:

  1. State dependent on society, entirely dependent on economically dominant class. (Communist Manifesto, 1848: 'executive of the modern state is but a committee for managing the common affairs of the whole bourgeoisie'). Capitalism is bourgeoisie; state starkly instrument for oppression of exploited class.
  2. More complex/subtle theory (e.g., analysis of revolutionary events 1848-51, Eighteenth Brumaire of Louis Bonaparte 1852): state could enjoy 'relative autonomy' from class system, capable of imposing will upon society, acting as 'appalling parasitic body'. Theory of state articulating interests of *any* class, not just bourgeoisie, but those of most populous class in French society, the smallholding peasantry. Did not develop this view in detail; autonomy relative, appears to mediate conflicting classes, maintains class system itself.

The Capitalist State (Continued)

Both theories differ markedly from liberal/pluralist models. Emphasize state cannot be understood except in context of unequal class power, arising out of, and reflecting, capitalist society. Either instrument of oppression wielded by dominant class, or (more subtly) mechanism through which class antagonisms ameliorated. Nevertheless, Marx's attitude towards state not entirely negative; argued could be used constructively during transition from capitalism to communism ('revolutionary dictatorship of the proletariat').

Overthrow of capitalism would see destruction of bourgeois state, creation of alternative, proletarian one.

Describing state as proletarian 'dictatorship', Marx utilized first theory: state as instrument through which economically dominant class (proletariat) could repress/subdue other classes. All states, from this perspective, class dictatorships. 'Dictatorship of proletariat' seen as means of safeguarding gains of revolution counter-revolution mounted by dispossessed bourgeoisie. Predicted as class antagonisms faded, state would 'wither away', resulting communist society would be stateless.

The Capitalist State (Neo-Marxism)

Since state emerged out of class system, once class system abolished, state loses reason for existence. Marx's ambivalent heritage provided modern Marxists/neo-Marxists considerable scope. Encouraged by writings of Antonio Gramsci, who emphasized degree to which ruling class domination achieved by ideological manipulation ('hegemony') rather than just open coercion. Bourgeois domination maintained largely through 'hegemony': intellectual leadership/cultural control, state playing important role.

Since 1960s, Marxist theorizing dominated by rival instrumentalist and structuralist views.

  • Instrumentalist (Miliband, 1969): State agent/instrument of ruling class. Extent state elite disproportionately drawn from privileged backgrounds emphasized. Bias derived from overlap of social backgrounds between state officials and elites in business, finance, etc.
  • Structuralist (Poulantzas, 1968): Dismissed sociological approach. Argued state cannot but act to perpetuate social system (capitalism). Structure of economic/social power exerts constraint on state autonomy.
In capitalist state, role is to serve long-term interests of capitalism, even if actions resisted by sections of capitalist class itself. Neo-Marxists see state as terrain on which struggle amongst interests, groups, classes conducted. Rather than instrument, state itself struggle.

4.4.3 The Leviathan State

Image of state as 'leviathan' (in effect, self-serving monster intent on expansion/aggrandizement) associated with New Right. Rooted in early/classical liberalism, particularly commitment to radical individualism.

New Right, or at least neoliberal wing, distinguished by strong antipathy towards state intervention in economic/social life. Born out of belief state is parasitic growth threatening individual liberty/economic security. Instead of impartial umpire/arbiter, state seen as overbearing 'nanny', desperate to interfere/meddle. Central feature: state pursues interests separate from society (setting it apart from Marxism), interests demand unrelenting growth in role/responsibilities.

New Right thinkers argue 20th-century tendency towards state intervention reflected popular pressure for economic/social security, need to stabilize capitalism against class tensions, internal dynamics of state apparatus. Explain expansionist dynamics by reference to demand-side and supply-side pressures.

  • Demand-side: Emanate from society itself, usually through electoral democracy mechanism. Argument: electoral competition encourages politicians to 'outbid' one another making promises of increased spending/government programs, regardless of long-term damage (higher taxes, inflation, 'crowding out' investment).
  • Supply-side: Internal to state apparatus. Explained in terms of institutions/personnel of state apparatus. Most influential: argument government oversupply thesis. Associated with public-choice theorists examining how public decisions made based on assumption individuals act rationally, self-interestedly. Bureaucratic self-interest invariably supports 'big' government/state intervention (enlarges bureaucracy itself, job security, pay, promotion prospects, status). Image of self-seeking bureaucrats at odds with pluralist notion of state machine imbued with ethic of public service subject to political control.

4.4.4 The Patriarchal State

Modern thinking about state must take account of feminist theory implications. Not to say there is single feminist theory; encompasses range of traditions, differing views towards state power.

Feminists usually not regarded nature of state power as central political issue, preferring focus on deeper structure of male power centered on institutions like family/economic system. Some feminists question conventional definitions of state, arguing idea state exercises monopoly of legitimate violence compromised by routine use of violence/intimidation in family/domestic life. Implicitly/explicitly, feminists helped enrich state debate developing novel/challenging perspectives.

Liberal feminists: Believe sexual/gender equality brought about through incremental reform; accept essentially pluralist view of state. Recognize if women denied legal/political equality (esp. right to vote), state biased in favor of men. Faith in state's basic neutrality reflected in belief any bias can/will be overcome by process of reform. Sense state can be used impartially to promote justice/common good. Often view state positively, seeing intervention as means of redressing gender inequality/enhancing role of women. Campaigns for equal-pay legislation, abortion legality, provision of child-care facilities, extension of welfare benefits.

The Patriarchal State (Radical Feminism)

Nevertheless, more critical/negative view developed by radical feminists, arguing state power reflects deeper structure of oppression in society at large (patriarchy). Parallels Marxist view of state power: both groups deny state autonomous entity bent on pursuit of own interests. Instead, state understood/its biases explained by reference to 'deep structure' of power in society at large.

Where Marxists place state in economic context, radical feminists place it in context of gender inequality; insist state essentially institution of male power.

Common with Marxism, distinctive instrumentalist and structuralist versions of feminist power developed:

  • Instrumentalist: Views state as little more than agent/'tool' used by men to defend interests/uphold structures of patriarchy. Line of argument draws on core feminist belief patriarchy rooted in division of society into distinct 'public' and 'private' spheres of life, men dominating former while women confined to latter. Quite simply, state run by men, for men.
  • Structuralist: Arguments focus on personnel of state (particularly state elite), emphasize degree state institutions embedded in wider patriarchal system. Modern radical feminists paid particular attention to emergence of welfare state, seeing it as expression of new kind of patriarchal power. Welfare upholds patriarchy bringing transition from private dependence (women as 'home makers' dependent on men as 'breadwinners') to public dependence increasingly controlled by institutions of extended state. Women have become clients or customers of state services (childcare, nursery education, social work) and employees, particularly in 'caring' professions (nursing, social work, education).

4.5 The Role of the State

Brainstorming Questions:

What should states do? What functions or responsibilities should the state fulfil, and which ones should be left in the hands of private individuals?

Contrasting interpretations have clear implications for desirable role/responsibilities. With exception of anarchists (dismiss state as fundamentally evil/unnecessary), all political thinkers regarded state as, in some sense, worthwhile. Even revolutionary socialists (inspired by Leninist slogan 'smash the state') accept need for temporary proletarian state to preside over transition from capitalism to communism ('dictatorship of the proletariat').

Nevertheless, profound disagreement about exact role state should play, proper balance between state and civil society. Different forms state systems developed:

  • Minimal states
  • Developmental states
  • Social-democratic states
  • Collectivized states
  • Totalitarian states
  • Religious states

4.5.1 Minimal States

Ideal of classical liberals, aim to ensure individuals enjoy widest possible realm of freedom. Rooted in social-contract theory, but advances essentially 'negative' view of state. Value of state: capacity to constrain human behavior, prevent encroaching on rights/liberties of others. State merely protective body; core function provide framework of peace/social order.

In Locke's view, state acts as 'night watchman' whose services needed only when orderly existence threatened. This implies state 'minimal' or 'night watchman' state with three core functions:

  1. Maintain domestic order.
  2. Ensure contracts/voluntary agreements made between private citizens enforced.
  3. Provide protection against external attack.
Institutional apparatus thus limited to police force, court system, military. Economic, social, cultural, moral responsibilities belong to individual, are therefore part of civil society.

Cause of minimal state taken up by New Right. Drawing on early liberal ideas, particularly free-market economic theories, they proclaimed need to 'roll back the frontiers of the state'.

Minimal States (New Right) & Developmental

In writings of Robert Nozick (1974), amounts to restatement of Lockean liberalism based on defense of individual rights, especially property rights. In case of free-market economists like Friedrich von Haye and Milton Friedman, state intervention seen as 'dead hand' reducing competition/efficiency/productivity. Economic role confined to two functions: maintenance of stable means of exchange ('sound money' - low/zero inflation) and promotion of competition (controls on monopoly power, price fixing).

4.5.2 Developmental States

Best historical examples minimal states were UK/USA during early industrialization. As general rule, later country industrializes, more extensive its state's economic role. In Japan/Germany, state assumed more active 'developmental' role.

Developmental state intervenes in economic life with specific purpose of promoting industrial growth/economic development. Does not amount to socialist system of planning/control but, rather, attempt to construct partnership between state and major economic interests, often underpinned by conservative/nationalist priorities. Classic example: Japan. During Meiji Period (1868–1912), state forged close relationship with Zaibatsu (great family-run business empires) that dominated economy up until WWII. Since 1945, developmental role assumed by Ministry of International Trade and Industry (MITI), which, together with Bank of Japan, helps shape private investment decisions/steer Japanese economy towards international competitiveness. Similar model existed in France, where governments (left/right) recognized need for economic planning, state bureaucracy custodian of national interest. Austria/Germany economic development achieved through 'partnership state', emphasis on close relationship between state/major economic interests (notably big business/organized labor).

Competition States & Social Democratic

More recently, economic globalization fostered emergence of 'competition states', examples found amongst tiger economies of East Asia. Competition states distinguished by recognition need to strengthen education/training as principal guarantee of economic success in context of intensifying transnational competition.

4.5.3 Social Democratic (Welfare) States

Whereas developmental states practice interventionism to stimulate economic progress, social-democratic states intervene with view to bringing about broader social restructuring, usually in accordance with principles like fairness, equality, social justice. Countries like Austria/Sweden, state intervention guided by developmental/social democratic priorities. Nevertheless, developmentalism/social democracy do not always go hand-in-hand. As Marquand (1988) pointed out, UK state significantly extended in period immediately after WWII along social-democratic lines, failed to evolve into developmental state. Key to understanding: shift from 'negative' view (state as little more than necessary evil) to positive view (state means of enlarging liberty/promoting justice). Social-democratic state thus ideal of both modern liberals/democratic socialists.

Rather than merely laying down conditions of orderly existence, social-democratic state active participant, helping rectify imbalances/injustices of market economy. Tends focus less upon generation of wealth, more upon what is seen as equitable/just distribution. Aims attempt to eradicate poverty/reduce social inequality. Twin features: Keynesianism and social welfare. Aim of Keynesian economic policies 'manage'/'regulate' capitalism promoting growth/maintaining full employment. Although may entail element of planning, classic Keynesian strategy involves 'demand management' through adjustments in fiscal policy (levels of public spending/taxation). Adoption of welfare policies led to emergence of 'welfare states', responsibilities extended to promotion of social well-being amongst citizens. State 'enabling state', dedicated to principle of individual empowerment.

4.5.4 Collectivized States

While developmental/social-democratic states intervene in economic life view guiding/supporting largely private economy, collectivized states bring entirety of economic life under state control. Best examples orthodox communist countries like USSR/throughout Eastern Europe. Sought abolish private enterprise altogether, set up centrally planned economies administered by network of economic ministries/planning committees ('command economies'). Justification stems from fundamental socialist preference for common ownership over private property. However, use of state to attain this goal suggests more positive attitude towards state power than outlined in classical writings of Marx/Engels.

Marx/Engels by no means ruled out nationalization; Engels recognized state control extended to include factories, banks, transportation. Nevertheless, envisaged proletarian state strictly temporary, would 'wither away' as class antagonisms abated. In contrast, collectivized state USSR became permanent, increasingly powerful/bureaucratic. Under Stalin, effectively equated with statism, advance of socialism reflected in widening responsibilities/powers of state apparatus. Indeed, after Khrushchev announced 1962 dictatorship of proletariat ended, state was formally identified with interests of 'the whole Soviet peoples'.

4.5.5 Totalitarian States

Most extreme/extensive form of interventionism found in totalitarian states. Essence construction of all-embracing state, influence penetrates every aspect of human existence. State brings not only economy, but also education, culture, religion, family life under direct state control. Best examples Hitler's Germany/Stalin's USSR, although modern regimes like Saddam Hussein's Iraq arguably similar. Central pillars comprehensive process of surveillance/terroristic policing, pervasive system of ideological manipulation/control.

In this sense, totalitarian states effectively extinguish civil society, abolish private sphere altogether. Goal only fascists, who wish dissolve individual identity within social whole, prepared openly endorse. Sometimes argued Mussolini's notion of totalitarian state derived from Hegel's belief state as 'ethical community' reflecting altruism/mutual sympathy of members. From this perspective, advance of human civilization linked to aggrandizement of state, widening responsibilities.

4.5.6 Religious States

On face of it, religious state contradiction in terms. Modern state emerged largely through separation between church and state. Advance of state sovereignty thus usually went hand in hand with forward march of secularization. In USA, secular nature enshrined in First Amendment; in France separation guaranteed freedom of worship shall not be abridged, while principle of *laïcité* maintained strict emphasis on separation. In countries like Norway, Denmark, UK, 'established' or state religions developed, although privileges generally enjoy stop well short of theocratic rule, political influence generally restricted by high level level of social secularization.

Nevertheless, period since 1980s witnessed rise of religious state, driven by tendency within religious fundamentalism reject public/private divide view religion as basis of politics. Far from regarding political realm as inherently corrupt, fundamentalist movements typically look seize control of state use it as instrument of moral/spiritual regeneration. Was evident, for instance, in process 'Islamization' introduced Pakistan under General Zia-ul-Haq after 1978, establishment 'Islamic state' Iran result of 1979 revolution. Close links between Sri Lankan state/Sinhala Buddhism, particularly during years violent struggle against Tamil separatism.

Although strictly speaking, religious states founded on basis of religious principles, and, in Iranian model, contain explicitly theocratic features, in other cases religiously-oriented governments operate context of constitutional secularism.

4.6.1 What is Government?

Broadest sense, government means to rule or control others. Can therefore be taken to include any mechanism through which ordered rule is maintained, its central features being ability make collective decisions and capacity enforce them. A form of government identified in almost all social institutions like families, schools, businesses, trade unions and so on. However, government in our context refers to formal/institutional processes that operate at national level maintain public order/facilitate collective action. It is body or organ that administers country/main organization dealing affairs of whole country. Thus, government one of most essential components, also administrative wing of state.

In other words, government can also refer political organization comprising individuals/institutions authorized formulate public policies/conduct affairs of state. Governments empowered establish/regulate interrelationships of people within territorial confines, relations community whole, dealings of community other political entities. Government applies both national states (federal government Ethiopia/governments subdivisions like regional states, provinces, municipalities) and international states. Any form of government, to be stable/effective, must possess two essential attributes: authority/legitimacy.

Authority and Legitimacy

Authority:

In politics, word authority implies ability compel obedience. Can simply be defined as 'legitimate power.' While power ability influence behavior, authority is right to do so. Therefore, based on acknowledged duty obey rather than coercion/manipulation. Thus, authority legitimacy, justification/right exercise that power. Authority can be expressed naked force/terror as case undemocratic governments or through series more transparent public hearings most democratic states.

Legitimacy:

Term legitimacy (from Latin *legitimare*, 'to declare lawful') broadly means rightfulness. Attribute of government prompts governed comply willingly its authority. Confers on order/commands authoritative or binding character, thus transforming power into authority. Legitimacy popular acceptance of governing regime/law as authority.

Considered basic condition rule; without at least minimal amount legitimacy, government deadlocks/collapses. As long as legitimacy stays certain level, stability maintained, fails below this level endangered. For most regimes seen requiring large proportion population retain power, but not necessarily case, since many unpopular regimes known survive provided they seen legitimate within small but influential elite. Therefore, legitimacy gained through acquisition power accordance recognized/accepted standards or principles. That say legitimate government 'do the right thing' therefore deserves be respected/obeyed. Concept legitimacy differs from legality in sense term legality does not necessarily guarantee government respected citizens acknowledge duty obedience.
