Defining Ethics

We all have some more or less intuitive understanding of what morality is about, but if asked to explain its nature, or the concepts in terms of which we make moral judgments, most people would be hard pressed to come up with satisfactory answers.

Ethics is a branch of philosophy which attempts to understand, interpret, and to some extent guide, the practice of morality.

The Task of Moral Philosophy

It gives us the clarity of thought and integrity of action in moral judgment. If moral philosophy cannot, in the end, tell us what to do, it can at the very least tell us what we mean when we claim to have done the morally right thing.

It can also tell us what the fundamental bases of such moral claims might be, and disclose to us various constellations of moral rules, principles, values, and approaches that might be used in making moral judgments.

It is philosophical thinking about, or analysis of, moral problems, dilemmas, and concepts, and the making of moral judgments. It seeks to impart understanding and clarity to a practice with which we are all, in one way or another, thoroughly familiar.

Moral and Non-Moral Judgments

Moral Judgment

Judgments of Obligation: These judgments arise when we intend to evaluate behavior or actions in some manner. They involve assessing whether a particular course of action is morally right or wrong, or whether it aligns with our duties and responsibilities. For example, stating that it is our obligation to be honest in our dealings reflects a judgment of obligation.

Non-Moral Judgments

  • Judgments of Taste: These judgments involve expressing one's satisfaction or dissatisfaction based on sensory experiences. They pertain to personal preferences regarding matters of aesthetics, such as food, music, fashion, or visual art. For example, declaring that a dish is delicious or a painting is visually appealing reflects a judgment of taste.
  • Aesthetic Judgments: Aesthetic judgments encompass the appreciation or critique of artworks, cultural artifacts, or natural beauty. They involve analysis and reflection on artistic qualities, symbolism, and emotional impact. For instance, when someone evaluates a novel as thought-provoking or a sculpture as evoking a sense of awe, they are making aesthetic judgments.
  • Prudential Judgments: Prudential judgments relate to evaluating what actions or decisions are rational for promoting one's well-being. These judgments consider the consequences, benefits, and risks associated with different choices. For example, pursuing higher education to enhance career prospects is a prudential judgment.
  • Legal Judgments: Legal judgments evaluate behavior based on legal obligations outlined in laws, regulations, or contracts. For example, when a judge assesses a defendant's guilt or innocence based on evidence and applicable laws, they are making a legal judgment.
  • Judgments of Value: These judgments ascribe goodness or value to things or attributes. They determine what is intrinsically valuable or admirable. For example, declaring honesty or kindness as valuable qualities is a judgment of value.
  • Judgments of Virtue: These judgments evaluate character traits or dispositions known as moral virtues, such as honesty, courage, or compassion. Recognizing someone as virtuous because of their consistent display of integrity is a judgment of virtue.

Transforming Non-Moral Judgments into Moral Judgments

Non-moral judgments can potentially transform into moral judgments when they involve interpersonal relationships and adopt a critical stance. For example, a non-moral judgment like, "John's taste in music is different from mine," can transform into a moral judgment if we say, "John's taste in music is terrible, and it shows a lack of cultural sophistication." The shift introduces a critique of John's character, thus turning the statement into a moral judgment.

Dimensions of Ethics

  • Descriptive Ethics: Ethics should not be confused with descriptive ethics, which is the objective observation and analysis of cultural values and behaviors. Sociological and historical descriptions of cultural values can provide valuable insights for moral philosophers, serving as inputs for their analysis and deliberations.
  • Mete-Ethics: Ethics also encompasses the study of the nature and significance of moral judgments, exploring the criteria and meaning behind our evaluations of right and wrong.
  • Normative Ethics: Focuses on elucidating the fundamental rules, principles, and values that serve as the basis for justifying moral judgments.
  • Applied Ethics: Examines specific moral practices, beliefs, and dilemmas in various fields and contexts, such as medical ethics, environmental ethics, or business ethics.

Meta Ethics

  • The term "meta" denotes a perspective that is beyond or transcendent, and thus, metaethics provides a detached and overarching examination of the entire scope of ethics.
  • Metaethics can be defined as the study of the origin and significance of ethical concepts, delving into their underlying nature and meaning.
  • In comparison to normative ethics and applied ethics, the field of metaethics is the least precisely delineated domain within moral philosophy.

Metaethics places two prominent issues at the forefront:

  • Metaphysical issues: These pertain to questioning whether morality exists independently of human beings, exploring the ontological status of moral principles and values.
  • Psychological issues: These concern the underlying cognitive and emotional foundations of our moral judgments and behavior, delving into the mental processes that shape our ethical decision-making.

Metaphysical Issues: Objectivism and Relativism

Metaphysics explores the various types of entities that exist within the universe. While certain entities, like rocks, are composed of physical matter, there may be others that possess a nonphysical nature, such as thoughts, spirits, and deities.

In the context of Metaethics, the metaphysical aspect revolves around investigating the nature of moral values. It seeks to determine whether moral values are eternal truths that exist in a realm similar to spirits or if they are merely human conventions, created and defined by society. This exploration delves into the fundamental ontological status of moral values, aiming to discern their ultimate nature and existence.

Proponents of the other-worldly view typically hold that moral values are objective in the sense that they exist in a spirit-like realm beyond subjective human conventions. They also hold that they are absolute, or eternal, in that they never change, and also that they are universal insofar as they apply to all rational creatures around the world and throughout time.

The most classic example of this view is Plato, who was inspired by the field of mathematics. When we look at numbers and mathematical relations, such as 1 + 1 = 2, they seem to be timeless concepts that never change and apply everywhere in the universe. Humans do not invent numbers, and humans cannot alter them. Plato explained the eternal character of mathematics by stating that they are abstract entities that exist in a spirit-like realm. He noted, in the same way, that moral values also are absolute truths and thus are also abstract, spirit-like entities. Medieval philosophers commonly grouped all moral principles together under the heading of “eternal law,” which were also frequently seen as spirit-like objects.

17th-century British philosopher Samuel Clarke described them as spirit-like relationships rather than spirit-like objects. In either case, though, they exist in a spirit-like realm.

A different other-worldly approach to the metaphysical status of morality is divine commands issuing from God’s will. Sometimes called voluntarism (or divine command theory), this view was inspired by the notion of an all-powerful God who is in control of everything. God simply wills things, and they become reality. According to the Divine command approach, God wills the physical world into existence, he wills human life into existence, and similarly, he wills all moral values into existence.

Proponents of this view, such as medieval philosopher William of Ockham, believe that God wills moral principles, such as “murder is wrong,” and these exist in God’s mind as commands. God informs humans of these commands by implanting us with moral intuitions or revealing these commands in scripture.

The second is this-worldly approach to the metaphysical status of morality. It follows in the skeptical philosophical tradition, such as that articulated by Greek philosopher Sextus Empiricus, and denies the objective status of moral values. Technically, skeptics did not reject moral values themselves but only denied that values exist as spirit-like objects, or as divine commands in the mind of God. Moral values, they argued, are strictly human inventions, a position that has since been called moral relativism.

There are two distinct forms of moral relativism:

  • Individual relativism (subjectivism): This holds that individual people create their own moral standards. Friedrich Nietzsche, for example, argued that the superhuman creates his or her morality distinct from and in reaction to the slave-like value system of the masses.
  • Cultural relativism: This maintains that morality is grounded in the approval of one’s society – and not simply in the preferences of individual people. This view was advocated by Sextus, and in more recent centuries by Michel Montaigne and William Graham Sumner.

Psychological Issues in Metaethics

A second area of metaethics involves the psychological basis of our moral judgments and conduct, particularly understanding what motivates us to be moral.

We might summarize this subject by asking the simple question, “Why be moral?”

Some answers to the question “Why be moral?” include:

  • To avoid punishment
  • To gain praise
  • To attain happiness
  • To be dignified
  • To fit in with society

Egoism and Altruism

Egoism and altruism are two opposing ends of a spectrum when it comes to motivation. They deal with why we do the things we do.

Egoism

Egoism is the belief that one's self-interest is the primary motivating factor in all human behavior. In other words, egoists believe that people ultimately act for their own benefit, even if it appears they are helping others. There are two main types of egoism:

  • Ethical egoism: This view says that acting in your own self-interest is the only rational thing to do. Proponents like Ayn Rand argue that cooperation with others can be a form of enlightened self-interest, but helping others for purely altruistic reasons is misguided.
  • Psychological egoism: This is a more descriptive theory, rather than a moral one. It suggests that all our actions, even seemingly selfless ones, are ultimately motivated by a desire for personal gain, such as a good feeling or social approval.

Altruism

Altruism is the opposite of egoism. It refers to the selfless concern for the welfare of others. Altruistic acts are done to benefit someone else, even at a cost to oneself. Helping a stranger in need, donating to charity, or sacrificing oneself to save another are all examples of altruism.

Questions for Discussion

  • Are humans naturally egotistical, or is altruism a learned behavior?
  • Is volunteerism truly altruistic, or is there an underlying benefit?
  • Can helping others ever be purely out of self-interest?

Cognitivism and Non-Cognitivism

Cognitivism and non-cognitivism are two fundamental opposing viewpoints in Metaethics. They fundamentally disagree on how moral statements function.

Cognitivism

Cognitivism argues that moral statements express beliefs about objective facts, similar to factual statements. These facts could be about the world, human nature, or God's will (depending on the specific type of cognitivism). Moral statements can therefore be true or false based on these underlying facts.

Here's an analogy:

Factual statement: The grass is green. (This is objectively true based on the properties of light reflecting off grass.)

Cognitivist moral statement: Stealing is wrong. (According to a cognitivist, this is true based on some underlying moral fact.)

Non-Cognitivism

Non-cognitivism rejects the idea that moral statements express beliefs or truths. They argue that moral statements serve a different purpose in language. Different forms of non-cognitivism propose alternative functions:

  • Emotivism: Moral statements express emotions or feelings ("Stealing is wrong!" expresses disapproval)
  • Prescriptivism: Moral statements are commands or prescriptions ("Don't steal!")

Strong Cognitivism: Naturalism

Strong Cognitivism: This aligns with regular cognitivism. It believes moral statements express genuine beliefs that can be true or false, just like factual statements. They represent some objective moral reality, not just personal opinions or emotions.

Naturalism: This distinguishes it from other cognitivist views. Naturalism argues that these objective moral facts are grounded in natural properties of the world, discoverable through science. Moral properties like "goodness" or "wrongness" are not in some separate realm but instead rooted in the natural world.

Analogy:

Factual statement: Water boils at 100°C at sea level. (This is a natural property)

Strong cognitivist naturalist moral statement: Causing unnecessary suffering is wrong. (The "wrongness" here is based on natural properties like the capacity for pain and the negative effects of suffering on well-being)

Thinkers Associated with Strong Cognitivism (Naturalism)

David Hume: He argued that moral judgments are based on sentiments or feelings that arise from our natural social experiences. For example, we find cooperation beneficial and harmful actions upsetting, leading us to judge them as good or bad.

Recently, the Cornell realists (a group of philosophers associated with Cornell University): Nicholas Sturgeon, Richard Boyd, and David Brink.

Criticisms of Strong Cognitivism (Naturalism)

The is-ought problem: How do "is" statements (descriptive facts about the world) translate to "ought" statements (moral judgments)? Just because something is a natural feature (e.g., humans feel pain) doesn't necessarily mean it dictates a moral obligation (e.g., to avoid causing pain).

The variety of moral views: If morality is grounded in one set of natural properties, how can there be so much disagreement about moral issues?

Strong Cognitivism: Non-Naturalism

Strong Cognitivism: This aligns with regular cognitivism. It believes moral statements express genuine beliefs that can be true or false, just like factual statements ("The Earth is round"). They represent some objective moral reality, not just personal opinions or emotions.

Non-Naturalism: Unlike some other cognitivist views (particularly naturalism), strong cognitivism with non-naturalism argues that these objective moral facts are not reducible to natural properties of the world we can discover through science. Moral properties like "goodness" or "wrongness" are fundamentally different and irreducible to things like pleasure, pain, or evolutionary benefits.

Analogy:

Factual statement: Sugar is sweet. (This is a natural property we can explain scientifically)

Strong cognitivist non-naturalist moral statement: Helping others in need is good. (The "goodness" here is not reducible to pleasure or evolutionary advantage, but a distinct moral property)

Thinkers Associated with Strong Cognitivism (Non-Naturalism)

G.E. Moore: A prominent philosopher who argued for the "open question argument." Simply put, even if something like pleasure is associated with good actions ("helping others makes you feel good"), it's still a meaningful question to ask whether the action itself is good, independent of the pleasure. This suggests "goodness" is a separate, non-natural property.

Criticisms of Strong Cognitivism (Non-Naturalism)

Difficulties in explaining the nature of non-natural properties: How do these non-natural moral properties exist and interact with the natural world?

Difficulties in grounding morality: If morality isn't based on anything observable or natural, how do we justify moral claims and reach objective moral truths?

Strong Cognitivism without Moral Realism: Mackie's 'Error-Theory'

According to Mackie, moral judgments are cognitively meaningful and can be evaluated as true or false. In this sense, he aligns with strong cognitivism. However, Mackie argues that all moral judgments are ultimately mistaken or "in error" because there are no objective moral facts in the world.

We lack a plausible way to access these moral facts: Unlike facts about the physical world (investigated by science), there's no clear method to verify or discover objective moral truths.

The nature of these moral properties seems strange: How would objective moral properties like "goodness" or "wrongness" exist independently of the world? They seem different from physical properties or mathematical truths.

So, if there are no objective moral facts, how can moral statements be true or false? This is the "error" part of the theory. Mackie argues that:

  • We still make moral judgments: We say things like "torture is wrong" and genuinely believe them.
  • These judgments aim to be true: We're not just expressing emotions or preferences; we believe we're capturing some kind of objective moral reality.
  • However, we're mistaken: Because there are no objective moral facts, our attempts to make true moral judgments are ultimately in error.

Criticisms of Mackie's Error Theory

Doesn't explain the persistence of morality: If morality is just a mistake, why is it so universal and persistent across cultures?

Difficulties with moral disagreement: If everyone is just making errors, how can we even have meaningful moral disagreements?

Weak Cognitivism about Morals without Moral Realism: Best Opinion Theories

Weak Cognitivism: This departs from strong cognitivism. Here, moral statements are still seen as attempts to represent some kind of objective reality, but not necessarily facts in the traditional sense. They might express:

  • Judgments: Our best judgments about what is right or wrong.
  • Beliefs: Beliefs about what would be morally best, even if there's no underlying moral reality.

Without Moral Realism: Similar to Mackie's error theory, best opinion theories reject the idea of objective moral facts existing independently of the world. There's no "moral truth" out there waiting to be discovered.

Best Opinion Theories: This is where the theory gets its name. It proposes that:

  • Moral statements aim to be correct: When we say "helping others is good," we're trying to express a well-founded moral judgment, not just personal preference.
  • The "correctness" is based on the best available reasoning: This "best" reasoning could involve factors like promoting human flourishing, reducing suffering, or adhering to widely accepted moral principles.
  • There might not be a single "best" answer: Moral issues can be complex, and there might be multiple reasonable perspectives. The "best opinion" could be context-dependent.

Thinkers associated with Best Opinion Theories:

  • David Wong: A contemporary philosopher who argues for a form of "reflective equilibrium" where we compare our moral intuitions with moral principles and revise both until we reach a stable and well-justified set of beliefs.

Criticisms of Best Opinion Theories

Difficulties in grounding "best": What makes some opinions about morality "better" than others? How do we resolve disagreements?

Potential for relativism: Can any opinion be considered "best" if there's no objective moral truth?

Best opinion theories offer a way to make moral judgments without relying on absolute moral truths. However, they face challenges in providing a clear standard for evaluating and resolving disagreements in morality.

Non-Cognitivism

Non-cognitivists deny that moral judgments are even apt to be true or false. Non-cognitivists thus disagree with both weak and strong cognitivism. It has four versions:

  • J. Ayer's emotivism (1936): According to this view, moral judgments express emotions or sentiments of approval or disapproval.
  • Simon Blackburn's quasi-realism (1984): This perspective holds that moral judgments express our dispositions to form sentiments of approval or disapproval.
  • Allan Gibbard's norm-expressivism (1990): This theory suggests that our moral judgments express our acceptance of norms. Moral judgments aren't about stating objective facts or simply expressing emotions. Instead, they function as a way to express our acceptance of a particular norm—a rule that governs how we should feel in certain situations. For example, saying "Stealing is wrong" isn't stating a universal truth but rather expresses your adherence to a norm that makes you feel guilty if you steal.

Prescriptivism advocated by Richard Hare maintains that moral judgments are prescriptions adopted by the moral agents. When someone makes a moral judgment, such as "Stealing is wrong," they are not only expressing their own personal disapproval of stealing but also prescribing or recommending that others should refrain from stealing. Hare argues that moral judgments are based on universalizable principles that can be applied consistently across different situations. Hare distinguishes between two types of moral language:

  • Descriptive language: Aims to describe or state facts about the world.
  • Prescriptive language: Involves making recommendations or issuing commands.

According to Hare, moral language falls into the latter category of prescriptive language.

Normative Ethics

Normative ethics involves:

  • A search for an ideal litmus test of proper behavior.
  • Arriving at moral standards that regulate right and wrong conduct.
  • Using rules that enable one to determine whether any possible action is right or wrong.

The key assumption in normative ethics is that there is only one ultimate criterion of moral conduct, whether it is a single rule or a set of principles.

Three normative ethics approaches are:

  • Virtue ethics
  • Duty ethics
  • Consequentialist ethics

Virtue Ethics

Many philosophers believe that morality consists of following precisely defined rules of conduct, such as “don’t kill,” or “don’t steal.” The central question of virtue ethics, however, is not “what should I do?”, but “what kind of person should I be?”

Virtue ethics places less emphasis on learning rules, and instead stresses the importance of developing good habits of character.

In addition to advocating good habits of character, virtue theorists hold that we should avoid acquiring bad character traits, or vices, such as cowardice, insensibility, injustice, and vanity.

Virtue theory emphasizes moral education since virtuous character traits are developed in one’s youth. Adults, therefore, are responsible for instilling virtues in the young.

Aristotle argued that virtues are good habits that we acquire, which regulate our emotions.

Analyzing 11 specific virtues, Aristotle argued that most virtues fall at a mean between more extreme character traits.

Aristotle's Virtues

Aristotle's list of virtues can be interpreted in different ways. Here are 11 virtues commonly attributed to his ethical framework:

  • Courage: The virtue of facing fear and taking appropriate risks in order to act morally and achieve noble goals.
  • Temperance: The virtue of moderation and self-control, avoiding excesses and finding a balanced approach to life's pleasures and desires.
  • Generosity/Liberality: The virtue of giving and sharing freely, both material possessions and one's time and energy, without expecting something in return.
  • Magnificence: The virtue of acting grandly in one's pursuits, particularly in matters of generosity and spending for the benefit of others.
  • Magnanimity: The virtue of having a great and noble soul, self-confidence, and a sense of honor and dignity.
  • Ambition: The virtue of desiring achievement, power, or recognition.
  • Patience: The virtue of maintaining composure and perseverance in the face of difficulties, delays, or provocation.
  • Truthfulness: The virtue of honesty and sincerity in one's words and actions, avoiding deceit and falsehood.
  • Wit: The virtue of possessing a good sense of humor and using it appropriately, bringing joy and lightness to social interactions.
  • Friendliness: The virtue of cultivating and maintaining positive relationships, being kind, pleasant, and considerate towards others.
  • Modesty: The virtue that involves an attitude of humility, restraint, and moderation in one's behavior, appearance, or achievements. It is the opposite of boastfulness or excessive self-promotion. Modesty typically involves downplaying one's strengths or accomplishments, showing respect for others, and avoiding arrogance or ostentation. Modesty can be seen as an expression of self-awareness and an acknowledgment of the contributions and worth of others.

Pros and Cons of Virtue Ethics

Pros

  • Emphasis on Moral Character: Virtue ethics places a strong emphasis on the development of virtuous character traits such as honesty, compassion, courage, and justice. It highlights the importance of cultivating these virtues in individuals, which can lead to the development of morally good and admirable persons.
  • Flexibility: Virtue ethics provides a flexible framework that allows for contextual and situational considerations. It recognizes that ethical decisions may vary depending on the circumstances and encourages individuals to exercise practical wisdom in applying moral virtues to specific situations.
  • Focus on Long-term Ethics: Unlike some other ethical theories that primarily focus on specific actions or rules, virtue ethics emphasizes the long-term development of moral character. It encourages individuals to cultivate virtuous habits and traits that will guide their ethical behavior consistently throughout their lives.
  • Holistic Approach: Virtue ethics takes a holistic approach to ethics, considering not only individual actions but also the overall character, intentions, and motivations of individuals. It recognizes that ethical behavior is not solely determined by isolated actions but by the broader ethical framework of a person's character.

Cons

  • Lack of Specific Guidelines: One criticism of virtue ethics is that it does not provide specific guidelines or rules for determining right or wrong actions in particular situations. Critics argue that this lack of clear guidance can lead to subjectivity and uncertainty in ethical decision-making.
  • Cultural Relativism: Virtue ethics can be susceptible to cultural relativism, as virtues and moral character traits may vary across different cultures and societies. Critics argue that this relativism can undermine the universality and objectivity of ethical principles.
  • Limited Application in Moral Dilemmas: Virtue ethics may face challenges when dealing with moral dilemmas that require immediate action or involve conflicting virtues. It may not offer clear guidance on how to prioritize virtues or resolve conflicts between different moral character traits.
  • Individual Focus: Some critics argue that virtue ethics places excessive emphasis on individual character development, potentially neglecting broader social and systemic factors that influence ethical behavior. This criticism suggests that virtue ethics may not adequately address issues of social justice and structural inequalities.

Deontological Ethics

Duty theories base morality on specific, foundational principles of obligation. Deontological is derived from the Greek word deon, or duty, in view of the foundational nature of our duty or obligation.

They are also sometimes called non-consequentialists since these principles are obligatory, irrespective of the consequences that might follow from our actions. For example, it is wrong to not care for our children even if it results in some great benefit, such as financial savings.

Deontological Ethical Approaches

There are six central deontological ethical approaches:

Divine Command Theory (DCT)

The first (earliest) approach to deontological ethics is the Divine Command Theory (DCT), discussed by Plato in his dialogue Euthyphro and later by Leibniz in his Discourse on Metaphysics.

According to Divine Command Theory, the true standard of moral right and wrong lies in the will of God.

Two Versions of Divine Command Theory

The DCT has two versions:

  1. First Version: This version posits that the basis for our moral claims is rooted in God's will or commands. It suggests that actions are morally right or wrong based on whether or not God has commanded them.
  2. Second Version (Divine Index Version): This perspective asserts that X is not morally right simply because God commanded it. Instead, God commands us to do X because it is already morally right in and of itself.

Critiques of Divine Command Theory

Critics of the first version argue that if moral standards are solely a result of God's arbitrary will, without any objective standards to which God adheres, then His commands become completely arbitrary. This raises concerns about the consistency and rationality of moral judgments derived from Divine Command Theory.

Critics of the Divine Index version argue that it provides limited practical guidance since determining the specific commands of God can be challenging. They question how we can accurately discern God's commands. Are they found in religious scriptures, sacred texts from various religions, or in the teachings of proclaimed "divinely inspired" individuals?

The Natural Law Approach

The second ethical approach to deontological ethics is the natural law approach as proposed by Thomas Aquinas. According to Aquinas, there exists a universal, unchanging, rational, and objective law that governs the entire universe, known as the Eternal Law. This Eternal Law encompasses both physical laws and natural laws.

Types of Laws

Physical laws govern the behavior of inanimate objects and the laws that dictate animal behavior. On the other hand, moral or natural laws govern the conduct of rational beings. Aquinas asserts that it is essential for natural laws to be promulgated or communicated. While physical laws are communicated through instincts, moral laws are communicated through decrees and proclamations.

Promulgation of Eternal Law

The part of the Eternal Law that pertains to human beings is promulgated in two distinct ways: through the Natural Law and the Divine Law. Aquinas believes that the Natural Law is inherently "imprinted on us" and self-evident, much like the law of non-contradiction or the principle of "Nihil ex Nihilo" (nothing comes from nothing). On the other hand, Divine Laws are communicated through various divine texts.

Categories of Natural Laws

Aquinas makes an important distinction between two categories of Natural Laws: primary principles and secondary principles. Among the primary principles is one basic principle from which all the other primary and secondary principles are thought by Aquinas to follow: The Fundamental Principle of the Natural Law (FP): That good is to be done and pursued, and evil is to be avoided.

Basic Inclinations

According to Aquinas, some of our very basic inclinations that we share with all other things in the universe include:

  • Preservation of our offspring
  • Education of children
  • Inclination to live in society

Primary vs. Secondary Principles

It is not easy to draw a clear demarcation between primary and secondary principles of natural law. However, secondary principles are characterized by:

  • Being more detailed and concrete than primary principles
  • Being more difficult to grasp
  • Being logically entailed by the primary ones
  • Ultimately being traceable back to FP

Whereas the primary principles are self-evident to all rational creatures, the secondary principles are “not equally known to all.” Secondary principles can be prevented from emerging to consciousness by "evil persuasions,” “vicious customs,” and “corrupt habits” or errors in reasoning.

Examples of Moral Issues

Homosexuality, abortion, and sexual activity outside marriage are instances which some cultures endorse but are considered immoral according to Aquinas. Thus, the need for Divine Law arises here.

Reasons for Divine Law

According to Aquinas, there are two main reasons why Divine Law is necessary:

  • Human beings have a supernatural end.
  • It serves as an essential corrective for our tendency to make errors in deriving secondary principles of the Natural Law from primary ones.

Pufendorf's Ethical Approach

The third approach is that championed by 17th-century German philosopher Samuel Pufendorf, who classified dozens of duties under three headings: duties to God, duties to oneself, and duties to others.

Duties to God

Concerning our duties towards God, Pufendorf argued that there are two kinds:

  • A theoretical duty to know the existence and nature of God.
  • A practical duty to both inwardly and outwardly worship God.

Duties to Oneself

Regarding our duties towards oneself, these are also of two sorts:

  • Duties of the soul, which involve developing one’s skills and talents.
  • Duties of the body, which involve not harming our bodies, as we might through gluttony or drunkenness, and not killing oneself.

Duties to Others

Concerning our duties towards others, Pufendorf divides these between:

  • Absolute Duties: These are universally binding on people, which include:
    • Avoiding wronging others.
    • Treating people as equals.
    • Promoting the good of others.
  • Conditional Duties: These arise from contracts between people, involving various types of agreements, the principal one of which is the duty to keep one’s promises.

Rights Theory in Deontological Ethics

The fourth approach to deontological ethics is the Rights Theory, which was proposed by John Locke. In this theory, a "right" refers to a justified claim against another person's behavior, such as my right to not be harmed by you.

Correlativity of Rights and Duties

Duty ethics in rights theory is based on the idea that rights and duties are interconnected in a way that the rights of one person imply the corresponding duties of another person. For instance, if I have a right to receive $10 from Smith, then Smith has a duty to pay me $10. This concept is known as the correlativity of rights and duties.

John Locke's Contributions

One of the most influential early formulations of rights theory comes from John Locke, a 17th-century British philosopher. Locke argued that the laws of nature dictate that we should not harm anyone's life, health, liberty, or possessions. According to Locke, these rights are inherent to us and are given by God.

Fundamental Rights

Building upon Locke's ideas, the United States Declaration of Independence, authored by Thomas Jefferson, acknowledges three fundamental rights: life, liberty, and the pursuit of happiness.

Characteristics of Moral Rights

Moral rights are characterized by four key features:

  • Natural Rights: Rights are considered natural because they are not invented or created by governments. They are seen as inherent and independent of human laws or regulations.
  • Universal Rights: These rights are regarded as universal since they are not subject to variation or alteration from one country to another. They are believed to apply universally to all individuals, regardless of geographical location or cultural context.
  • Equal Rights: Moral rights are deemed equal, meaning that they are the same for all people without discrimination based on gender, race, or disabilities. Every individual is entitled to these rights on an equal basis.
  • Inalienable Rights: These rights are considered inalienable, implying that they cannot be transferred or relinquished to another person. For example, an individual cannot sell themselves into slavery or give up their rights willingly.

W.D. Ross's Duty-Based Ethics

The six duty-based ethics, developed by British philosopher W.D. Ross, places emphasis on prima facie duties. In line with his predecessors from the 17th and 18th centuries, Ross argues that our duties are inherent to the fundamental nature of the universe. However, Ross's list of duties is considerably shorter, aimed at reflecting our actual moral convictions. These are known as Ross's prima facie duties.

Prima Facie Duties

Prima facie duties are conditional or provisional moral obligations that hold unless overridden by other conflicting duties. They represent a set of general principles that guide ethical decision-making but can be superseded in certain circumstances. Actual duties, on the other hand, are the specific moral obligations that are binding in a particular situation. They are determined by considering the relevant factors and weighing conflicting obligations.

Ross's Prima Facie Duties

  • Fidelity: The duty to uphold and keep promises.
  • Reparation: The duty to provide compensation or make amends when we harm others.
  • Gratitude: The duty to express appreciation and gratitude to those who help us.
  • Justice: The duty to recognize and give due consideration to merit and fairness.
  • Beneficence: The duty to actively improve the conditions and well-being of others.
  • Self-improvement: The duty to cultivate and enhance our own virtue and intelligence.
  • Nonmaleficence: The duty to refrain from causing harm or injury to others.

Critical Assessment of W.D. Ross's Duty-Based Ethics

The inclusion of self-improvement on a list of moral duties can be subject to debate. While neglecting one's talents or allowing health to deteriorate may be considered imprudent or unwise, it may not necessarily be deemed immoral. The question arises as to whether personal development and well-being should be categorized as moral obligations or fall under the realm of personal choices and prudence.

Similarly, the duty to be grateful can be seen as questionable. If a favor is done with the expectation of something owed in return, it may not truly be a selfless act of kindness but rather a transaction or investment. Gratitude, in such cases, may be more of a social norm or a matter of personal preference rather than a moral duty.

Another critical point challenges Ross's notion of moral intuition. According to Ross, the existence of prima facie duties is self-evident to any rational human being who seriously contemplates the requirements of morality. However, if a principle is genuinely self-evident, it would be expected that everyone would universally agree on its validity. The fact that there can be disagreement and differing perspectives suggests that the self-evidence of these duties may not be as universally apparent as Ross claims.

Pros and Cons of Deontological Ethics

Pros

  • Emphasis on Moral Duties: Deontological ethics places a strong emphasis on moral duties and obligations. It provides a clear and structured framework for determining right and wrong based on adherence to these duties. This can provide individuals with a sense of moral clarity and guidance in decision-making.
  • Objective Standards: Deontological ethics relies on objective moral standards and rules that are independent of personal desires or consequences. It prioritizes the intention and nature of actions rather than their outcomes. This objectivity can provide a stable foundation for ethical reasoning and decision-making.
  • Respect for Autonomy: Deontological ethics respects individual autonomy and the inherent worth of human beings. It holds that individuals have inherent rights and dignity that should be protected, regardless of the potential consequences or utility of their actions. This can promote principles of human rights and equality.

Cons

  • Lack of Flexibility: Deontological ethics can be criticized for its rigidity and lack of flexibility. It may not readily accommodate situational or contextual factors that could impact moral decision-making. Critics argue that a strict adherence to moral rules may lead to moral absolutism and an inability to adapt to complex moral dilemmas.
  • Conflict of Duties: In certain situations, deontological ethics can lead to conflicts between moral duties. When faced with conflicting duties, it may be challenging to determine the appropriate course of action. Resolving these conflicts can be subjective and may require further moral reasoning or the prioritization of certain duties over others.
  • Lack of Consideration for Consequences: Deontological ethics focuses primarily on the nature of actions and adherence to moral rules, often disregarding the potential consequences of those actions. Critics argue that this neglect of consequences can lead to moral decisions that are detached from real-world outcomes and fail to consider the overall well-being or utility of individuals affected by those actions.
  • Limited Moral Guidance: While deontological ethics provides a framework based on moral duties, it may not offer comprehensive guidance in all moral situations. It may not address complex ethical dilemmas that require a more multifaceted consideration of consequences, virtues, or other moral factors.

Consequentialism / Teleological Ethics

Consequentialism, as a moral theory, asserts that the rightness or wrongness of an action is solely determined by the balance of its consequences. According to consequentialism, once we have considered all the consequences, we determine whether the total good consequences outweigh the total bad consequences. If the overall benefits exceed the drawbacks, the action is deemed morally correct. Conversely, if the negative consequences outweigh the positive, the action is considered morally improper.

Consequentialism: Ethical Egoism, Altruism, and Utilitarianism

Ethical Egoism

Ethical egoism argues that what makes an action morally right is whether it benefits you, the individual. It promotes pursuing your own good as the ultimate moral obligation.

Different Forms:

  • Individual Egoism: This holds that everyone should prioritize their own well-being above all else, even if it harms others.
  • Personal Egoism: This version focuses on the individual's self-interest but doesn't make claims about what others ought to do.
  • Universal Egoism: This proposes that everyone acting in their own best interest leads to the greatest good for all.

Arguments For:

  • Natural: Proponents argue that self-preservation and promoting your own well-being are natural instincts.
  • Motivation: Focusing on self-interest can be a strong motivator for achievement and personal growth.

Arguments Against:

  • Selfishness: Critics see it as inherently selfish, potentially leading to a society where everyone prioritizes themselves over others' needs.
  • Ignorance of Others: It might lead to neglecting the well-being of others and the importance of social cooperation.
  • Unrealistic: Universal egoism, where everyone acts solely in their own interest, might create chaos and undermine social structures that benefit everyone.

Comparison with Other Theories:

  • Deontology: Deontological ethics, like Kant's theory, focuses on duty and following moral rules, regardless of consequences. This stands in contrast to egoism's focus on self-interest.
  • Consequentialism: Consequentialism judges actions based on outcomes. While some consequences of an action might benefit you, egoism goes further, making your benefit the sole moral consideration.

Ethical Altruism

Ethical altruism proposes that individuals have a moral obligation to act for the benefit of others, even if it comes at a cost to themselves. The well-being of others becomes the primary factor in determining the morality of an action.

Focus on Duty

Similar to deontological ethics, ethical altruism emphasizes acting according to a moral code, but this code prioritizes helping others over personal gain.

Arguments For:

  • Promotes Well-being: A society where people prioritize helping others can lead to greater overall happiness and well-being for everyone.
  • Moral Obligation: Some argue we have a moral duty to help those in need, stemming from empathy and compassion.
  • Combats Selfishness: Ethical altruism encourages acting beyond self-interest and fostering a sense of social responsibility.

Challenges and Considerations:

  • Defining "Benefit": What constitutes "good" for others can be subjective and complex.
  • Self-Sacrifice: There's a potential risk of neglecting your own needs entirely, leading to burnout or resentment.
  • Finding Balance: Striking a balance between helping others and taking care of yourself is crucial.

Comparison with Other Theories:

  • Deontology: While both emphasize duty, deontology focuses on following universal moral laws, which might not always prioritize the good of others.
  • Consequentialism: Consequentialism judges actions based on outcomes, and helping others can be a positive outcome, but it's not the sole factor.

Utilitarianism

Utilitarianism focuses on maximizing happiness and well-being for the greatest number of people.

Key Aspects of Utilitarianism:

  • Greatest Happiness Principle: This core principle states that the morally right action is the one that produces the greatest amount of good or happiness for the greatest number of people affected by the action.
  • Utilitarian Calculus: Ideally, a utilitarian would weigh the potential happiness and unhappiness caused by each option before acting. Hedonistic calculus, as invented by Bentham, takes into consideration factors such as intensity, duration, fecundity, extent, propinquity, purity, and certainty of happiness/suffering.
  • Jeremy Bentham and John Stuart Mill: These two philosophers are the most famous proponents of utilitarianism. Bentham focused on maximizing pleasure and minimizing pain, while Mill considered the quality of happiness as well as the quantity.

Example of Applying Utilitarianism:

Imagine a city is considering a loud but effective new airport. Utilitarians would consider:

  • Increased happiness for travelers due to efficiency.
  • Decreased happiness for residents due to noise pollution.

The action (building the airport) would be considered morally right only if the overall increase in happiness for travelers outweighs the decrease in happiness for residents.

Types of Utilitarianism

There are different types or variations of utilitarianism, each with its own features and approaches. The main types of utilitarianism include:

Act Utilitarianism

Act utilitarianism, also known as classical utilitarianism, focuses on evaluating the moral rightness or wrongness of individual actions. According to act utilitarianism, an action is morally right if it maximizes overall happiness or utility in a particular situation. It emphasizes the immediate consequences of each action and requires a case-by-case analysis to determine the morally correct course of action. The major proponent is J. Bentham.

Rule Utilitarianism

Rule utilitarianism emphasizes the importance of following general rules or principles that, when consistently applied, lead to the greatest overall happiness or utility. Instead of evaluating each individual action, rule utilitarianism focuses on the consequences of adopting and adhering to certain moral rules. It argues that these rules are generally reliable in promoting overall well-being, even if there may be occasional exceptions. The major proponent is J.S. Mill.

Social Contract Approach to Ethics

Hobbes, a proponent of psychological egoism, argued that all of our actions are motivated by self-interest. Building upon this foundation, he developed a normative theory known as social contract theory.

According to Hobbes, individuals have a selfish motive to live in a world with moral rules rather than one without them. In the absence of moral rules, we are vulnerable to the self-interested actions of others. Our property, families, and even our lives are constantly at risk. Recognizing this, each self-interested individual is motivated to adopt a basic set of rules that facilitate a civilized community.

As inherently selfish creatures, we would be inclined to exploit our neighbors' property when we perceive an opportunity. However, this would put us at risk from our own neighbors. Therefore, out of self-interest, we establish a system to enforce these rules: a policing agency that punishes those who violate them.

Morality, in this sense, arises out of the social contract, where individuals surrender certain rights and freedoms to a sovereign authority in exchange for the benefits of a stable and secure society.

In Hobbes' social contract theory of morality, individuals willingly surrender most of their natural rights to liberty to a sovereign authority, except for their right to self-defense.

David Gauthier's Version of the Social Contract Approach

Prisoner's Dilemma

Consider the following scenario: You and your friend have each been apprehended and charged in connection with the same crime and are taken to separate rooms to be interviewed. In the course of your interrogation, you learn the following facts:

If you confess to the crime but your friend does not, you will get a one-year prison sentence. Your friend, on the other hand, will be put away for ten years. You also learn that if you do not confess while your friend does, it will be you who gets the ten-year sentence and your friend who gets off with only one year. Furthermore, if both of you confess to the crime, each of you will get five years in prison; and if neither of you confesses, the authorities will have enough to put each of you away for only two years.

Finally, although you are unable to communicate with your friend, you do know that he is rational and that he has managed to piece together the same information as you have. In this scenario, you and your friend are faced with what is known as the Prisoner’s Dilemma.

Gauthier's Perspective on the Prisoner's Dilemma

According to Gauthier, the structural problem inherent in the Prisoner’s Dilemma reveals an important fact: the decision to confess only appears to be rational according to a strategy Gauthier calls straightforward utility maximization.

Morality facilitates, or makes possible, the rational pursuit of constrained maximization, which leaves everyone better off.

Pros and Cons of Consequentialism

Pros

  • Focus on Outcomes: Consequentialism prioritizes the actual outcomes of actions, emphasizing the practical consequences they produce. This approach can be seen as practical and pragmatic, as it directs attention to the real-world impact of our choices.
  • Flexibility and Contextual Consideration: Consequentialist theories often allow for flexibility in decision-making, taking into account the specific circumstances and context of each situation. This adaptability can be advantageous in addressing complex moral dilemmas that require a nuanced evaluation of consequences.
  • Promotes the Greater Good: Consequentialism aims to maximize overall well-being or utility. By focusing on promoting the greatest good for the greatest number of people, it seeks to foster positive outcomes and societal welfare.

Cons

  • Ethical Dilemmas and Trade-Offs: Consequentialism may require making difficult ethical choices that involve trade-offs between competing values.   • Measurement and Predictability Challenges: Assessing and predicting the consequences of actions accurately can be complex and challenging. It may be difficult to quantify or predict the long-term impact of an action, making it more challenging to make precise moral judgments.
  • Neglect of Moral Intentions and Duties: Critics argue that consequentialism places too much emphasis on outcomes, potentially neglecting the moral intentions of individuals or the importance of moral duties and obligations. It may overlook the intrinsic value of certain actions or the significance of adhering to moral principles, focusing solely on the consequences.
  • Lack of Moral Constraints: Some argue that consequentialism lacks inherent moral constraints, potentially justifying morally questionable actions if they lead to desirable outcomes. This can raise concerns about the potential for overlooking important ethical considerations or engaging in morally objectionable behavior.

Applied Ethics

Applied ethics refers to a branch of ethics that focuses on the examination and analysis of specific moral dilemmas that are controversial in nature. These dilemmas encompass a range of topics, including but not limited to abortion, animal rights, and euthanasia.

Over time, applied ethics has been categorized into various subfields for convenience, such as medical ethics, business ethics, environmental ethics, and sexual ethics.

Criteria for Applied Ethical Issues

For an issue to be considered an "applied ethical issue," it must meet two criteria:

  • First, the issue needs to be controversial in the sense that there are significant groups of people both for and against the issue at hand.
  • The second requirement for an issue to be an applied ethical issue is that it must be a distinctly moral issue.

Examples of Controversial Issues

Issues such as:

  • Affirmative action policies
  • Involuntary commitment in the military
  • Capitalistic versus socialistic business practices
  • Public versus private health care systems
  • Energy conservation

Although all of these issues are controversial and have an important impact on society, they are not all moral issues. They are only issues of social policy.

The aim of social policy is to help make a given society run efficiently by devising conventions, such as traffic laws, tax laws, and zoning codes. Moral issues, by contrast, concern more universally obligatory practices, such as our duty to avoid lying. Moral issues are not confined to individual societies.

Normative Principles in Applied Ethics

The following principles are the ones most commonly appealed to in applied ethical discussions:

  • Personal benefit: Acknowledge the extent to which an action produces beneficial consequences for the individual in question.
  • Social benefit: Acknowledge the extent to which an action produces beneficial consequences for society.
  • Principle of benevolence: Help those in need.
  • Principle of paternalism: Assist others in pursuing their best interests when they cannot do so themselves.
  • Principle of harm: Do not harm others.
  • Principle of honesty: Do not deceive others.
  • Principle of lawfulness: Do not violate the law.
  • Principle of autonomy: Acknowledge a person’s freedom over his/her actions or physical body.
  • Principle of justice: Acknowledge a person’s right to due process, fair compensation for harm done, and fair distribution of benefits.
  • Rights: Acknowledge a person’s rights to life, information, privacy, free expression, and safety.

The above principles represent a spectrum of traditional normative principles and are derived from both consequentialist and duty-based approaches.

The first two principles, personal benefit and social benefit, are consequentialist since they appeal to the consequences of an action as it affects the individual or society.

The remaining principles are duty-based. The principles of benevolence, paternalism, harm, honesty, and lawfulness are based on duties we have toward others.

The principles of autonomy, justice, and the various rights are based on moral rights.
