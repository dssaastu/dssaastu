Chapter Two: Approaches to Ethics

2.1 Chapter Introduction

Human beings ask questions about nature of morality. In the process of prescriptive inquiry, we employ a specific vocabulary. We also invoke theories to explain the nature of morality. All moral theories address the questions of what is Good, why it's Good, and where the Good is located?

If there is anything "easy” about moral inquiry it's the fact that there are only three basic kinds of prescriptive moral theories: teleological theories, deontological theories, and virtue-based theories. Unfortunately, they often (but not always) provide different and mostly conflicting answers to these basic questions. This Chapter aim to introduce you to various ethical theories.

2.2 Chapter Objectives

After reading this chapter, students will be able to:

  • Discuss ideas, feelings and questions about activities regarded as right or wrong, good or bad.
  • Explain why there are particular rules about what is right or wrong, good or bad behavior for different groups and situations.
  • Analyze the ethical dimensions of various rules and codes of behavior.
  • Examine the personal and community factors involved in defining beliefs about what is right or wrong, good or bad behavior.
  • Analyze how different contexts and situations influence personal values, attitudes, beliefs and behaviors.
  • Critically analyze how groups justify particular actions and behaviors.

2.3 Normative Ethics

We may now begin our review of problems and views in the area of normative ethics, starting with the theory of obligation and then going on to the theory of moral value and, finally, to the theory of nonmoral value. The ultimate concern of the normative theory of obligation is to guide us in the making of decisions and judgments about actions in particular situations. A main concern is to guide us as agents deciding what we should do.

But we also wish to make judgments about what others should do, what should have been done, or whether an action was right or wrong. We are agents, spectators, advisers, instructors, judges, and critics. In all these roles, our primary question is: how may or should we decide or determine what is morally right for a certain agent (oneself or another) to do, or what he morally ought to do, in a certain situation?

Normative ethics:

  • Offers theories or accounts of the best way to live. These theories evaluate actions in a systematic way, focusing on outcomes, duties, or motivation to justify human conduct.
  • Includes ethical theories like utilitarianism, deontology, virtue ethics, principlism, narrative ethics, and feminist ethics.

Normative ethics poses questions like:

  • Are there general principles or rules to distinguish right and wrong?
  • Are there virtues and/or relationships we can nurture to behave well?

2.3.1 Teleological Ethics (Consequentialist)

What is teleological/Consequentialist ethics?

It is referred as “the end justifies the means”. It believes in purpose, ends or goals of an action. It stresses that the consequences of an action determine the morality or immorality of a given action. An action is judged as right or wrong, moral or immoral depending on what happens because of it. One may have the best intention or follow high moral principles, but if the result is harmful or bad, the act is judged morally or ethically wrong.

Moral philosophers offer alternative standards to prevailing moral rules. These generally fall into two sorts: (1) deontological theories and (2) teleological ones.

A teleological theory says the basic or ultimate criterion of what is morally right, wrong, obligatory, etc., is the non-moral value brought into being. The final appeal must be to the comparative amount of good produced, or the comparative balance of good over evil.

  • An act is right if and only if it or its rule produces (or probably will/is intended to) at least as great a balance of good over evil as any alternative.
  • An act is wrong if and only if it does not.
  • An act ought to be done if and only if it or its rule produces (or probably will/is intended to) a greater balance of good over evil than any alternative.

2.3.1 Teleological Ethics (Continued)

For a teleologist, the moral quality/value of actions, persons, or traits depends on the comparative nonmoral value they bring about (or try to). Making moral quality depend on the moral value it promotes would be circular. Teleological theories make the right, obligatory, and morally good dependent on the nonmorally good.

To know if something is right, ought to be done, or is morally good, one must first know what is good in the nonmoral sense and whether the thing in question promotes it.

Teleologists may hold various views on what is nonmorally good.

  • Hedonists: Identify good with pleasure and evil with pain. The right action/rule produces the greatest balance of pleasure over pain.
  • Non-hedonists: Identify good with power, knowledge, self-realization, perfection, etc.
The key is that the teleologist has *some* view of good/bad and determines right/obligatory by what is conducive to the greatest balance of good over evil.

Deontological vs. Teleological Ethics

Deontological theories deny what teleological theories affirm. They deny that right, obligatory, and morally good are wholly a function of nonmoral good or maximizing the balance of good over evil (for self, society, or world). They assert other considerations make an action/rule right or obligatory besides consequences – e.g., features of the act itself (keeping a promise, justice, divine command).

Teleologists believe the sole ultimate right-making characteristic is the comparative nonmoral value produced. Deontologists deny this, insisting there are other basic right-making characteristics. For them, maximizing good over evil is either not a moral standard or not the *only* basic one.

A deontologist contends an action/rule can be morally right/obligatory even if it doesn't promote the greatest balance of good over evil. It might be right due to some other fact or its own nature.

Forms of Teleological Ethics (differ by 'whose good'):

  • Ethical Egoism: Promote one's own greatest good (Epicurus, Hobbes, Nietzsche). An act/rule is right iff it promotes at least as great a balance of good over evil for *oneself* in the long run as any alternative.
  • Ethical Universalism (Utilitarianism): Promote the greatest general good (Bentham, Mill). An act/rule is right iff it is conducive to at least as great a balance of good over evil in the *universe as a whole* as any alternative.
  • Intermediate theories: Promote good for a specific group (nation, class, family, race).
  • Ethical Altruism: Promote the good of *other* people.

We will focus on egoism and universalism (utilitarianism).

2.3.2 Egoism: Ethical and Psychological

2.3.2.1 Ethical Egoism

We usually assume morality involves not being overly concerned with oneself; selfishness is seen as unacceptable. However, disagreement exists about what constitutes ethical behavior.

Since early Western history, the viewpoint that humans are *not* built to look out for others' interests has surfaced. Some argue proper moral conduct is "looking out for number one." These are known as psychological egoism and ethical egoism.

Focusing on consequences (ends justify means) doesn't necessarily imply egoist consequences (maximizing one's own happiness). For example, Machiavelli argued for using any means (force, deceit) to maintain political power for oneself/party. This is Consequentialist but not utilitarian (lacks common good as ultimate end).

Dear Students, would you give your view on the following case?

Some years ago, a Good Samaritan stopped to help a man whose car had broken down. The man shot and killed the Samaritan, stole his car, and led police on a high-speed chase until killed. Most admire the Samaritan, but the ethical egoist would say the Samaritan did the wrong thing. For ethical egoism, the only rule is: Look after yourself. Stopping was throwing his life away.

2.3.2.1 Ethical Egoism (Continued)

This theory is called ethical egoism because it's a normative theory about how we *ought* to behave. It implies we ought to be selfish, or self-interested. "Ethical" here just means it's a theory advocating egoism as a moral rule.

"You should look after yourself"

The ethical egoist insists not taking advantage is foolish. Morality results from self-interest: if I mistreat others, they mistreat me, so I behave myself. This is a twisted version of the Golden Rule ("Do unto others as you would have them do unto you"), slanted towards self-interest. We treat others well *so that* we might receive similar treatment – we do it for ourselves.

Argument from Psychological Egoism:

  1. We all always seek to maximize our own self-interest (definition of psychological egoism).
  2. If one cannot do an act, one has no obligation to do that act (ought implies can).
  3. Altruistic acts involve putting others' interests ahead of our own (definition of altruism).
  4. But, altruism contradicts psychological egoism and so is impossible (by premises 1 and 3).
  5. Therefore, altruistic acts are never morally obligatory (by premises 2 and 4).

Ethical Egoism Clarifications & Application

An ethical egoist might help a stranded motorist, not for the motorist's sake, but to ensure "what goes around, comes around." The Golden Rule emphasizes others; ethical egoism emphasizes the self. Any theory looking solely at consequences is Consequentialist.

Ethical egoism advises doing what provides long-term benefit to oneself (exercise, healthy eating, etc.). Others' interests are unimportant unless helping them advances one's own interests (helping your children because you love *them*, but not the neighbor's children unless you gain gratification).

This leads to a rewritten Golden Rule: It's okay to treat others advantageously to yourself (and not them) if you can get away with it.

Method to Apply Ethical Egoism:

  • List the possible acts.
  • For each act, see how much net good it would do *for you*.
  • Identify the act that does the most net good *for you*.

Important points about ethical egoism:

  • Says *only* one's own welfare counts morally.
  • Doesn't forbid helping or require harming others, but the reason must stem from how it helps *you*.
  • Doesn't say always do what's most pleasurable; self-interest might require pain/sacrifice.

2.3.2.2 Psychological Egoism

The main basis for ethical egoism is often psychological: an argument from human nature. It's claimed we are constituted to always seek our own advantage/welfare, or what we *think* gives the greatest balance of good over evil. Butler called this "self-love" as the only basic principle. Contemporary terms include "ego-satisfaction" as the final aim or the "pleasure principle" as the basic drive.

If this psychological claim is true, the argument goes, our moral theory must recognize it. Our basic ethical principle must be self-love (perhaps "cool" self-love). Anything else flies in the face of facts.

One might object that we can't logically infer an ethical conclusion (ought) from a psychological premise (is). However, the egoist might argue: if human nature *is* purely self-interested, it's unrealistic and unreasonable to propose we *ought* to do anything else. Since "ought" implies "can," and we supposedly *can* only act self-interestedly (except by mistake), ethical egoism is the only reasonable approach.

Thus, ethical egoism generally presupposes psychological egoism: that each of us *is* always seeking our own greatest good (pleasure, happiness, power, etc.). The question isn't whether egoism is strong, but whether we *ever* desire others' welfare for its own sake, not just as a means to our own welfare.

Arguments Against Psychological Egoism

Counterarguments (often attributed to Bishop Butler) against psychological egoism:

  1. The desire for one's own good presupposes more basic desires (food, fame, sex). Without these "primary appetites," there'd be no "good" to be concerned about. Our welfare *is* the satisfaction of such desires.
  2. It follows that the object of basic desires isn't one's own welfare, but the specific things (food, fame). One's own good (self-love) is just *one* desire among many, not the object of *all* desires.
  3. Sometimes the object of a basic desire is for oneself (e.g., food). But it could be for someone else (e.g., enjoying the sight of the ocean, wanting someone else to enjoy something). Altruistic impulses (and desiring the right) might exist empirically.
  4. As a matter of fact, such altruistic interests *do* exist (as does malevolence – desiring another's pain for its own sake), and a desire to do the right.

The Psychological Egoist's Rebuttal and Counter-Rebuttal:

The egoist might say: "Yes, we do things for others, but we get *satisfaction* from it, and that satisfaction is our real end. Doing the 'altruistic' thing is just a means to our own satisfaction/good."

The counter: Of course we get satisfaction, but we don't do the act *because* of the expected satisfaction; we get satisfaction *because* we wanted to do the act in the first place. The egoist confuses the *object* of the desire (e.g., Person A enjoying the ocean) with the *satisfaction* that results for Person B when the object is attained. If B fails to get A to the ocean, B feels frustration *because* his goal was for A to enjoy himself, not because B's goal was frustration.

Generally, Egoistic and particularistic consequentialism consider only effects on oneself or a limited group (family, citizens, race). Moral rightness depends on consequences for the individual agent or group.

2.3.3 Utilitarianism: Producing the Best Consequences

"That action is best, which procures the greatest happiness for the greatest numbers."

Activity:

Suppose you are at Jigjiga with a dying millionaire. His last wish: give his $2 million to the Ethiopian Coffee Club (football) so they can buy better players. You promise. Later, in Addis Ababa, you see an ad from the Ethiopian Red Cross Society (ERCS) pleading for $2 million to save 100,000 people from starvation and build a sustainable economy. You trust ERCS's integrity. You reconsider your promise.

What is the right thing to do in this case?

Consider traditional moral principles:

  • "Let your conscience be your guide." Problem: Consciences differ. If yours says give to the club and mine says give to ERCS, discussion ends.
  • "Do whatever is most loving." Problem: Whom to love – the millionaire or the starving? Love alone doesn't settle conflicts. Is love always required (e.g., towards enemies, Hitler)?
  • Golden Rule: "Do to others as you would have them do to you." Problem: Needs modification ("...if you were in their shoes"). Still doesn't specify whom to prioritize here.

Conscience, love, Golden Rule are worthy rules of thumb but limited in complex cases with conflicting interests.

Deontology vs. Teleology in the Dilemma

A more promising strategy uses definite moral rules/principles.

If you decided to give the money to the Ethiopian Coffee Club to keep your promise (following the principle "Always keep your promise"), you adhered to deontology. Deontological systems value the act/kind of act itself; certain features have intrinsic value (e.g., lying is intrinsically wrong).

If you decided to give the money to ERCS to save lives and restore solvency, you sided with teleological ethics (consequentialism). The center of value is the outcome/consequences. Lying might be judged right/wrong based on the consequences it produces.

We examined one teleological ethic: ethical egoism (right act produces most good for the agent).

Unlike egoism, utilitarianism is a *universal* teleological system. It calls for maximizing goodness in *society* – the greatest goodness for the greatest number – not just the agent's good.

2.3.3.1 Classic Utilitarianism

We use utilitarian reasoning often. Formally, seeds were sown by Epicurus (342–270 BCE): "pleasure is the goal... the standard by which we judge everything good." Rightness/wrongness depends on pleasure/pain produced. Epicurus focused on individual experience (a form of egoism).

Epicurus inspired 18th-century philosophers emphasizing *general* happiness – pleasing consequences impacting others.

The classical expressions are from English philosophers/reformers Jeremy Bentham (1748–1832) and John Stuart Mill (1806–1873). They were nonreligious, optimistic humanists focused on moral and legal reform, impatient with rule-bound systems, seeking to make law serve human needs and interests.

2.3.3.2 Jeremy Bentham: Quantity over Quality

Bentham articulated two main features of utilitarianism:

  • The consequentialist principle (teleological aspect): Rightness/wrongness is determined by the goodness/badness of results. The end justifies the means.
  • The utility principle (hedonic aspect): The only thing good in itself is some state like pleasure, happiness, or welfare.

Hedonistic Utilitarianism & Hedonic Calculus

Hedonistic utilitarianism views pleasure as the sole good, pain as the sole evil. An act is right if it brings more pleasure than pain or prevents pain; wrong if it brings more pain than pleasure or prevents pleasure.

Bentham invented the hedonic calculus for measuring pleasure/pain. The quantitative score for any experience sums seven aspects:

  1. Intensity
  2. Duration
  3. Certainty
  4. Nearness (propinquity)
  5. Fruitfulness (fecundity - tendency to lead to more pleasures)
  6. Purity (tendency not to be followed by pains)
  7. Extent (number of people affected)

By adding up pleasure/pain scores for all possible acts and comparing, we decide which to perform. In our example, if giving to famine victims causes 3 million "hedons" (happiness units) and giving to the club causes < 1,000 hedons, we *must* give to the victims.

Appeal of Bentham's utilitarianism:

  • Simple (one principle: maximize pleasure, minimize suffering).
  • Commonsensical (morality seems about reducing suffering, promoting benevolence).
  • Scientific (quantitative measurement, impartial application - no special treatment for self/others based on race, gender, relationship, religion).

2.3.3.3 John Stuart Mill: Quality over Quantity

To counter objections that utilitarianism was a "pig philosophy" (valuing only base pleasures), Bentham's successor, J.S. Mill, distinguished happiness from mere sensual pleasure. His version is often called eudaimonistic utilitarianism (from Greek *eudaimonia*, "happiness").

Mill defined happiness via higher-order pleasures/satisfactions: intellectual, aesthetic, social enjoyments, and minimal suffering.

  • Lower (elementary) pleasures: Eating, drinking, sexuality, resting, sensuous titillation. More intensely gratifying, but can lead to pain if overindulged.
  • Higher pleasures: High culture, science, intellectuality, creativity. More long-term, continuous, gradual.

Mill argued higher pleasures are superior: “It is better to be a human being dissatisfied than a pig satisfied; better to be Socrates dissatisfied than a fool satisfied." Humans require more for true happiness: lower pleasures plus deep friendship, intellect, culture, art, knowledge, wisdom.

The quality of higher pleasures is better. Happiness is "not a life of rapture; but moments of such, in an existence made up of few and transitory pains, many and various pleasures, with a decided predominance of the active over the passive, and having as the foundation... not to expect more from life than it is capable of bestowing."

Mill emphasizes higher qualities (knowledge, intelligence, freedom, friendship, love, health), perhaps moving beyond pure pleasure to a non-hedonic cultivated state of mind.

2.3.3.4 Act- and Rule-Utilitarianism

Two classical types:

Act-utilitarianism (e.g., Bentham): Ideally, apply the principle of utility to *all* alternatives in *each* moment.
Definition: An act is right if and only if it results in as much good as any available alternative.
Problems:

  • Practicality: Cannot do the calculations for every act; need rules of thumb (e.g., "Don't lie," "Keep promises"). But the *right* act is still the one maximizing utility.
  • Intuition: Seems to violate fundamental intuitions about correct behavior (e.g., could justify punishing the innocent if it maximized utility).

Rule-utilitarianism (elements in Mill): Focuses on rules.
Definition: An act is right if and only if it is required by a rule that is itself a member of a set of rules whose acceptance would lead to greater utility for society than any available alternative.
Rationale: Humans are rule-followers. We need action-guiding rules. Act-utilitarian rule ("maximize utility") is too general. We often lack time to calculate consequences (e.g., lying vs. truth-telling), so need specific rules (like truthfulness) that pass the utility test.

Activity:

Debates continue. Trolley problem: You are driving a trolley, brakes fail. Ahead are 5 workers. You can switch to a sidetrack with 1 worker. What should you do?

The Strengths of Utilitarianism

Three positive features:

  1. Single principle, absolute system: "Do what will promote the most utility!" Offers a potential answer for every situation. Simple, action-guiding principle (though application can be hard).
  2. Gets to the substance of morality: Not just formal guidelines, but a material core: promote human (and animal?) flourishing, reduce suffering. Appeals to sense that morality is *for* people, about helping and alleviating suffering. Seems commonsensical (e.g., helping Kitty Genovese, giving millionaire's money to starving vs. football club).
  3. Addresses the problem of posterity: Why preserve resources for future generations? Egoism struggles here. Utilitarianism's duty is to maximize *general* happiness. If future life quality is positive, we have an obligation to continue human existence and ensure high quality of life. This implies obligations to future people: simplify lifestyles, conserve resources, protect species, reduce emissions, preserve wilderness.

Criticism of Utilitarianism (1)

Utilitarianism faces standard objections.

Problems with Formulating Utilitarianism

The formulation "The greatest happiness for the greatest number" has two variables ("happiness," "number"), leading to conflict.

Dear Students;

Example: $1,000 prize for running the longest distance in the shortest time. Abebe runs 5 km in 31 min; Kelbesa runs 7 km in 50 min; Obang runs 1 km in 6 min. Who wins? Abebe is longest distance, Obang is shortest time.

This mirrors the utilitarian problem:

  • Maximize the *number* getting happiness (spread it widely)? Might lead to procreating large populations.
  • Maximize the *total amount* of happiness? Might allow some to be much happier if their increase offsets losers' diminished happiness.
Should we prioritize total happiness or highest average happiness?

The Comparative Consequences Objection

Requires superhuman ability to foresee all consequences. We normally don't know long-term consequences, which extend indefinitely.

Criticism of Utilitarianism (2)

The Consistency Objection to Rule-Utilitarianism

Does rule-utilitarianism collapse into act-utilitarianism or become deontological? Argument: If following rules yields 100 hedons, but breaking a rule in a specific case (e.g., lying to spare feelings) yields 102 hedons without decreasing the total, shouldn't we break the rule? We could always improve rule-utilitarianism by breaking rules when utility increases, effectively making it act-utilitarianism. Thus, it may be inconsistent or lack an independent standard.

The No-Rest Objection

We must always do the act promoting the *most* utility. There's always something better we could be doing (helping homeless instead of cinema, saving ozone instead of sleeping). Should give away all assets beyond bare minimum. Utilitarianism seems to allow little rest or enjoyment if sacrificing increases others' happiness.

The Publicity Objection

Moral principles should be public knowledge for all to obey. But utilitarians might hesitate to recommend everyone act as a utilitarian (esp. act-utilitarian) due to complex deliberation needed. Might be better if most acted as deontologists. Seems to contradict publicity requirement.

Criticism of Utilitarianism (3)

The Relativism Objection

Rule-utilitarianism might seem relativistic, endorsing different rules in different societies (e.g., polygamy vs. monogamy; "Don't waste water" in desert vs. plentiful areas). But this isn't conventional relativism; the rule isn't valid by community choice, but by the *actual situation* and utility.

Criticism of the Ends Justifying Immoral Means

Chief criticism: Utilitarian ends could justify immoral means (deceit, torture, slavery, killing minorities) if the larger populace benefits.
Argument:

  1. If a moral theory justifies universally deemed impermissible actions, it must be rejected.
  2. Utilitarianism justifies such actions.
  3. Therefore, utilitarianism must be rejected.

The Lying Objection

W.D. Ross: Utilitarianism counterintuitively endorses lying if it serves the greater good. If Act A (lie) and Act B (truth) both yield 100 hedons, utilitarianism sees them as equal value. Implausible; truth seems intrinsically good.

Utilitarian reply: Truthfulness likely promotes overall welfare (needed for reliable info) and would be a rule-utility. But where lying *clearly* promotes utility without undermining the rule (e.g., lying to a gangster, white lies), we should lie.

Criticism of Utilitarianism (4): The Justice Objection

Utilitarian response on truth/integrity is intensified when considering justice, which most think shouldn't be compromised.

Example 1 (Sheriff): Rape/murder in racially volatile town. To prevent deadly riot and preserve harmony, sheriff could frame an innocent tramp. Speedy trial/execution likely stops riot. Only sheriff (and real criminal) know truth. Utilitarian seems committed to framing the tramp; many find this appalling.

Example 2 (Physician): Utilitarian physician has 5 patients needing organs (heart, lungs, liver, kidneys). Healthy bachelor comes for immunization. Utility-calculus suggests killing bachelor and using organs saves 5 lives, maximizing good.

These careless views of justice (misusing legal/medical systems) seem frightening.

However, utilitarians can't exclude sacrificing innocents for greater good. Would we sacrifice one innocent to prevent enormous evil (e.g., nuclear bomb destroying NYC)? If only way is torturing maniac's 10-year-old daughter, would we?

Utilitarianism: Justice Response & Action Formula

Rule-utilitarian response to torture example: Two rules conflict (prevent harm vs. against torture). Appeal to higher-level rule: "Sacrifice innocent person to prevent significantly greater social harm." Or, if no rule, use remainder rule: "Do act maximizing utility." This could justify torture.

Thus, justice is just another (lower-order) principle within utilitarianism. The difficulty lies in determining *which* wide-scale disasters warrant sacrificing innocents (e.g., wartime bombings overriding noncombatant distinction; deaths from driving automobiles). These judgment calls highlight utilitarianism's difficulty with justice.

Three-Step Action Formula:

  1. Project consequences of each alternative option (based on current knowledge).
  2. Calculate likely happiness (or balance over unhappiness) produced by each.
  3. Select the action producing the greatest happiness for the greatest number affected.

Utilitarianism considers consequences for *all* affected parties. Moral rightness depends on consequences for all affected people/sentient beings.

The principle of utility:

  • The morally right action produces the best overall consequences regarding utility/welfare of all affected parties.
  • Bentham's slogan: Right act/policy causes 'greatest happiness of the greatest number' (maximizes total utility/welfare).

2.3.3.5 Altruism

Question for Students:

'The end justifies the means'. Does this policy allow morally reprehensible acts for good ends?

In altruism, an action is right if consequences are favorable to all *except* the actor.

Bishop Butler argued for inherent psychological capacity for benevolence (psychological altruism): at least some actions are motivated by instinctive benevolence.

Psychological altruism (all human action is other-centered) is the opposite of psychological egoism. If both theories can be argued consistently, the soundness of one or both is questionable.

Example: Degu (poor swimmer) saves a child from drowning in Lake Tana. Is his ultimate motivation his own benefit? Seems odd, given the risk.

Altruists act to increase others' pleasure, even if it decreases their own pleasure or causes pain.

2.3.4 Deontological Ethics (Non-Consequentialist)

Differentiating egoistic/altruistic desires:

  • Desire is egoistic iff it concerns one's own perceived benefit, not anyone else's.
  • Desire is altruistic iff it concerns the perceived benefit of at least someone else.
Altruists reject psychological egoism, arguing humans are instinctively benevolent, which is the basis of altruistic moral obligations.

Deontology: What duty asks of us?

What makes a 'right' act right? Utilitarian/consequentialist: the good outcome. Deontological theory is often diametrically opposite.

Deontology is referred as “the means justifies the end”. Coined as “deontics”. Rightness/wrongness determined partly by formal rules of conduct, *not* consequences. Emphasis on intentions, motives, moral principles, performance of duty, rather than results. Consequences have nothing to do with rightness/wrongness.

Performance of One's own Duty

17th-century German philosopher Samuel Pufendorf classified duties under three headings:

  • Duties to God
  • Duties to oneself
  • Duties to others

Types of Duties & Divine Command Theory

Pufendorf's Duty Categories:

  • Towards God: (1) Theoretical duty to know God's existence/nature; (2) Practical duty to worship inwardly/outwardly.
  • Towards oneself: (1) Duties of the soul (develop skills/talents); (2) Duties of the body (not harming body, not killing oneself).
  • Towards others:
    • Absolute duties (universally binding): (1) Avoid wronging others; (2) Treat people as equals; (3) Promote others' good.
    • Conditional duties (result of contracts): Principal one is keeping promises.

2.3.4.1 The Divine Command Theory (DCT)

View that ethical principles are simply God's commands. Validity derives from God commanding them; they *mean* "commanded by God." Without God, no universally valid morality.

Three Theses of Strong DCT:

  1. Morality (rightness/wrongness) originates with God.
  2. Moral rightness = "willed by God"; moral wrongness = "against God's will."
  3. Morality based on divine will, not independent reasons; no further reasons needed.

Modified DCTs drop/qualify theses. Strong form implies: Act A is forbidden/required iff God commands not-A/A. Act A is permitted iff God commands neither A nor not-A.

Implications:

  1. Act A is wrong iff contrary to God's command.
  2. Act A is right (required) iff commanded by God.
  3. Act A is morally permissible iff permitted by God's command.
  4. If no God, nothing is ethically wrong, required, or permitted (moral nihilism).

Problems with DCT & Rights Theory

Problems with the Divine Command Theory

  1. Redundancy of "God is good": If "good" = "what God commands," then "God is good" means "God does what God wills" or "God practices what he preaches." It doesn't ascribe a property *to* God. "God commands us to do good" becomes the empty "God commands us to do what God commands us to do."
  2. Arbitrariness of morality: If God's decree is the sole arbiter, heinous acts (rape, innocent killing, cruelty) could logically become morally good if God suddenly commanded them. Morality seems arbitrary.

2.3.4.2 Rights Theory

A second duty-based approach. A "right" is a justified claim against another's behavior (e.g., my right not to be harmed by you).

Correlativity of rights and duties: Rights of one imply duties of another (e.g., If I have right to $10 payment from Smith, Smith has duty to pay me $10).

Most influential early account: 17th-century British philosopher John Locke. Laws of nature mandate we not harm anyone's life, health, liberty, or possessions. These are natural rights given by God.

US Declaration of Independence (Jefferson, following Locke) recognizes foundational rights: life, liberty, pursuit of happiness. Others deduced: rights to property, movement, speech, religion.

Features of Moral Rights & Kant Intro

Four features traditionally associated with moral rights:

  • Natural: Not invented/created by governments.
  • Universal: Do not change country to country.
  • Equal: Same for all people (irrespective of gender, race, handicap).
  • Inalienable: Cannot hand over rights to another (e.g., selling self into slavery).

2.3.4.3 Kant's Categorical Imperative

German philosopher Immanuel Kant (1724-1804) is identified with deontology. Adamantly opposed to outcomes determining moral worth.

For deontologists, it's the intention of the person, not consequences, that determines rightness/wrongness. Emphasis on correctness of action, regardless of benefits/harm. Some moral obligations are absolutely binding, regardless of consequences.

The Categorical Imperative

Kant's duty-based theory emphasizes a single principle. He agreed we have duties (develop talents, keep promises). But argued for a more foundational principle encompassing particular duties: a single, self-evident principle of reason called the "categorical imperative."

Categorical vs. Hypothetical Imperatives

A categorical imperative is fundamentally different from hypothetical imperatives that hinge on personal desire (e.g., "If you want a good job, then go to college"). A categorical imperative mandates action irrespective of desires (e.g., "You ought to do X").

Kant emphasized good intention (good will). Nothing is good in itself except a "good will." Intelligence, judgment, etc., are good only if the will using them is good.

By will, Kant means the uniquely human capacity to act according to concepts behind laws (principles). A good will acts according to nature's laws. A will is good without qualification only if its principle is: can the maxim of its action become a universal law?

This standard is crucial. Kant believed there was one categorical imperative (universal command) from which all duties derive: Act in such a way that the maxim or general rule governing our action could be a universal law.

Example: Making a promise you intend to break. Maxim: "Break promises when convenient." Could this be universal? No. It involves a contradiction of wills: you will to make promises (relying on the institution) and simultaneously will to break them (undermining the institution). Universal promise-breaking would dissolve the institution. Kant appeals to logical consistency, not bad consequences (unlike utilitarianism).

Kant gives three versions/formulations of the categorical imperative. It's a deontological theory: based on objective ethical rules. Relies on idea all people are capable of reasoning similarly.

Kantianism & Autonomy

Kantianism focuses on intent and action itself, not consequences like utilitarianism.

Hypothetical imperatives tell us means to achieve ends, but not which ends to choose. Dichotomy: "right" ends (helping someone) vs. "good" ends (enriching oneself). Kant considered "right" superior; "good" was morally irrelevant.

Right/moral conduct cannot be decided empirically. Judgments must be *a priori* (using pure practical reason). Reason, separate from experience, determines the moral principle: the categorical imperative. It dictates what *ought* to be done without reference to empirical factors. Moral questions determined independently of the subject. Because determined by pure reason, morality is universally valid (moral universalism) – impacting concepts of human rights and equality.

Kant's theory hinges on autonomy and the categorical imperative. Unless an action is chosen freely/willingly, it has no meaning (or moral value).

  • Autonomy: Allows self-creation of values/morality. One's own beliefs, independence, government; acting without regard for others.
  • Heteronomy: Acting under others' influence; allows placing blame externally.

Kant believed individuals are rational, capable of free choice (autonomous thinking). All humans, using reason on moral dilemmas, would agree on the Categorical Imperative (CI). The law is objective, but all can understand/agree via autonomous reflection. How does CI work?

Kant's Decision Procedure & Intent

The decision procedure is straightforward: When considering a moral action, ask:

  1. What would happen if everyone did this, all the time?
  2. Would that be the kind of world I'd like to live in?

Example: Murder. If everyone started killing, chaos ensues. Not a desirable world. Therefore, murder is wrong.

A core aspect is intent. Intentions matter to Kant.

Example: Murderer sees defenseless man, has knife. Considers killing. Decides not to kill – *not* because it's immoral, but due to risk of getting caught (self-preservation).
Kant: You haven't acted ethically. Your action (or non-action) doesn't make you better. You didn't make a *moral* choice.

However, if you chose not to kill because you realized it was *wrong* and didn't want to act unethically, *then* you acted morally and became a better person.

Kant: A true moral proposition must be untied to particular conditions/identity of the deliberator. A moral maxim implies *absolute necessity*, disconnected from physical details, applicable to any rational being. Leads to first formulation...

CI Formulation 1: Universality & Duty Types

A. The Principle of Universality

"Act only according to that maxim whereby you can at the same time will that it should become a universal law without contradiction."

Choose 'codes of conduct' only if they serve perfect/imperfect duty and are good for all. Kant divides duties from this formulation into two subsets:

  • Perfect duties: Blameworthy if not met. Basic requirements for humans. We have a perfect duty *not* to act by maxims resulting in logical contradictions when universalized.
    Example: "It is permissible to steal." Universalized, notion of property disappears, so stealing becomes impossible. Maxim logically negates itself.
    Example: Avoidance of suicide. Suicide ends life; self-love impels improving life. Contradiction.
  • Imperfect duties: Receive praise if completed, no blame if not. Circumstantial (e.g., cultivating talent). Based on pure reason but allow desires in practice. Not as strong as perfect duties, but still morally binding. Never truly completed. Differentiates from perfect duties because you can't constantly perform them.
    Example: Perfecting writing ability.

CI Formulation 2: Humanity as End

B. The Principle of Humanity as an End, Never as Merely a Means

"Act in such a way that you treat humanity, whether in your own person or in the person of any other, never merely as a means to an end, but always at the same time as an end."

Every rational action has a principle and an end. Most ends are subjective (pursued based on hypothetical imperatives). An objective end must be pursued categorically.

This principle has widespread approval. People, as rational beings, are ends in themselves, never to be used *merely* as means. Physical things can be means; using people merely as means (slavery, prostitution, exploitation) degrades them.

Also regulates actions affecting oneself. Suicide is wrong; treats my life as means to alleviate misery. Free will is source of rational action; treating it as subjective end denies freedom. Kant derives this second formulation from first.

Combining formulations: We have *perfect duty* not to use humanity (self/others) *merely* as means. Example: Slavery. Slave owner has right to property, but not to own a person. Owning contradicts autonomy/free will, violating first maxim.

Always treat people with dignity, never as mere instruments. Treating as end = reflecting their inherent value (e.g., donating to charity). Treating as means = using as tool for something else (e.g., stealing neighbor's car for my happiness - wrong).

CI Formulation 3: Autonomy & Rigidity

C. The Principle of Autonomy

"Therefore, every rational being must so act as if he were through his maxim always a legislating member in the universal kingdom of ends."

A truly autonomous will isn't subject to external interests, only laws it makes for itself. But these laws must be universalizable (as if binding others). Kant presents hypothetical "Kingdom of Ends" where all consider themselves both means and ends. Our actions affect everyone; should benefit all universally.

Recap Formulation 2: "Act... treat humanity... always... as an end and never merely as a means." We have perfect duty not to act by maxims creating incoherent states when universalized; imperfect duty not to act by maxims leading to unstable/undesirable states.

Problem: Rigidity

Famous example: Crazed axe-murderer at door asks where your children are. Lying seems right, but universal lying is bad world. CI says you can't lie. Tell truth? Kant said yes, immoral to lie (but could lock door/call police).

Another example: Man holds gun to mother's head. Button next to you drops him into spike pit, saving her. CI says wrong thing to do (can't press button). Mother dies?

Strict ethical systems struggle with complex exceptions. Morality too complex for these theories to fully work.

B. Agree consequences aren't fully controlled & shouldn't count in moral appraisal?

2.3.4.4 Ross's Prima Facie Duties or Moral Guidelines

Fourth duty-based theory: British philosopher W.D. Ross (1877–1971), emphasizing *prima facie* duties. (*Prima facie* = "at first sight" or "on the surface").

Prima facie duties: Duties dictating what we should do when other moral factors aren't considered. Generally obligate us, but might not in a specific case due to circumstances.

Actual duty: The action one *ought* to perform after considering and weighing *all* relevant prima facie duties.

Ross: Several prima facie duties help determine our concrete duty. A prima facie duty is binding (obligatory) *other things equal* (unless overridden/trumped by another duty). Strong presumption in its favor.
Example: Duty to keep promises. Ought to keep promise unless stronger moral considerations override.

Our actual or concrete duty is what we *should* perform in the specific situation. Morally bound to perform it. Prima facie duties relate to actual duties like reasons relate to conclusions.

Term "duty" in "prima facie duty" is slightly misleading. They are guidelines, not exceptionless rules. Not following a guideline isn't necessarily violating a rule. But failing to do one's *actual* duty *is* violating a rule (the rule corresponding to the actual duty in that case).

Ross's List of Prima Facie Duties

Ross argues duties are "part of the fundamental nature of the universe." His list reflects actual moral convictions:

  • Duties of Fidelity: Keep promises, don't lie/deceive.
  • Duties of Reparation: Make up for injuries done to others ("resting on a previous wrongful act"). Compensate harm caused.
  • Duties of Gratitude: Thank/help those who help us. (E.g., helping a friend who was previously selfless).
  • Duties of Justice: Act so benefits/burdens distributed fairly. Ross emphasizes negative aspect: duty to upset/prevent distributions not according to merit (pleasure/happiness). Includes preventing unjust distribution.
  • Duties of Beneficence: Improve conditions of others (foster health, security, wisdom, goodness, happiness). "Rests upon the fact that there are other beings... whose condition we can make better..."
  • Duties of Self-improvement: Act to promote one's own good (health, security, wisdom, goodness, virtue, intelligence, happiness).
  • Duties of Non-maleficence: Non-injury. Don't harm others physically/psychologically (health, security, intelligence, character, happiness). Obliged to avoid hurting others.

(Jacques Thiroux added duty to *prevent* injury by others, distinct from Ross's non-injury which is about *not causing* harm intentionally/negligently/ignorantly).

Summary of Ross & Virtue Ethics Intro

Ross presents seven categories (maybe more). Insists we accept them based on intuition, not primarily reason/argument. When duties conflict, the more obligatory one is our actual duty. Actual duty has greatest prima facie rightness over wrongness.

2.3.5 Virtue Ethics

Virtue Ethics: Challenging the adequacy of rule-based theories

"Virtue ethics" is a normative theory focused on virtues or moral character, distinct from deontology (rules/duties) and consequentialism (outcomes).

Example: Helping someone in need.

  • Deontologist: Emphasizes acting per rule ("Do unto others...").
  • Consequentialist: Points to maximizing well-being consequences.
  • Virtue ethicist: Emphasizes providing help would be charitable/benevolent (virtues).

2.3.5.1 Aristotle's Ethics

Ancient Greek philosopher Aristotle (384-322 B.C.) detailed virtue morality in *Nicomachean Ethics*. Understood 'virtus' as strength; virtues are strengths of character. Virtue theory later overshadowed by utilitarianism/deontology.

Resurfaced in past 50 years (Philippa Foot, Alasdair MacIntyre, Elizabeth Anscombe). Disillusioned with mainstream theories, argue virtue-based theory better answers "how ought we live?" than calculating consequences or obeying rules.

Key questions in virtue ethics:

  • What sort of person do I want to be?
  • What virtues characterize that person?
  • What actions cultivate those virtues?
  • What actions characterize that sort of person?

Good, right, happiness: Good isn't a disposition, involves teleological system involving actions.
A. Good is what all things aim at. Something good performs its proper function (good cup, good oak). Right action conduces to good; different goods for different sciences/arts. "The good" (best good) desired for own sake; for humans, this is eudaimonia (activity of soul per *arete* - excellence/virtue). *Eudaemonia* = living/doing well.
B. Human good not solved with mathematical exactitude (math starts general, argues to conclusions; ethics starts specific, argues to general). Ethics starts with actual moral judgments. Aristotle assumes natural tendencies.

Aristotle: Happiness, Virtue, Character

C. Aristotle distinguishes happiness (*eudaimonia*) and moral virtue:

  • Moral virtue not end of life (can coexist with inactivity, misery).
  • Happiness (end of life) is activity according to reason (human *arete*/excellence).
    • Happiness involves both moral and intellectual *arete*.
    • Some external goods necessary for this activity.

The Good Character

A. People have natural capacity for good character, developed through practice. Capacity doesn't come first.

ACT(S) ---> HABIT(S) ---> CHARACTER ---> STATE OF MIND (DISPOSITION) ---> ACT(S) ...
  • Sequence raises question of preeminence (acts vs dispositions). Interaction broken by Aristotle: acts *create* good dispositions; acts *flow from* good dispositions once created.
  • *Arete* (virtue/excellence) is disposition developed from capacity by proper exercise.
  • Habits developed through acting; character is structure of habits, formed by what we do.

B. Virtue (*arete*) defined as a mean between two extremes (excess and defect) regarding feeling/action, as practically wise person (*phronimos*) would determine. Mean not calculated *a priori*.

The mean is relative to individual and circumstances.

  • Level of courage differs for philosopher, commando, programmer.
  • Phronesis (practical wisdom): Ability to see the right thing to do in circumstances. Theory not relativistic; appropriate standards exist.
  • Ontological dimension: virtue is a mean. Axiological (value) dimension: virtue is an extreme/excellence. (MLK Jr. quote on being "extremist" for love/justice).

Pleasure cannot be directly sought (side-product of activity, element of happiness). Good person (*eudaimonia*) is standard for truly pleasant/unpleasant.

Aristotle: Friendship & Contemplation

IV. Friendship: Relationship to friend same as to oneself. Friend is second self.
A. In friendship, person loves himself (egoism) not by seeking money, but by giving money away to receive honor.
B. Kinds of friendship:

  • Utility
  • Pleasure
  • The Good (endures as long as both retain character)

V. The Contemplative Faculty: Exercise of perfect happiness in intellectual/philosophic activity.
A. Reason is highest faculty; can engage longer than other activities.
B. Philosophy loved as end-in-itself; *eudaimonia* implies leisure/self-sufficiency for contemplation.

Aristotle on Pleasure (Summary)

  1. *Eudaimonia*: Personal wellbeing, self-worth, zest for life, happiness, "good spirit".
  2. Happiness is activity of soul per *arete* (living/doing well). (Compare Maslow/Jung).
  3. Good is what performs proper function (*arete* established by purpose - teleological). Good person? Activity of soul per reason. Activity both moral (right thing/time) & intellectual (*phronesis*). External goods needed.
  4. Moral virtue not end (can coexist with misery).
  5. Good for person depends on circumstances (*i.e.*, initial conditions). Mean isn't relativism but applied to specifics (diet for weightlifter vs ballerina).
  6. Pleasure is side-product of activity; results from unimpeded activity. Neither good/bad, but positive effect perfecting activity. Not sought for own sake (hedonistic paradox).

2.4 Non-Normative Ethics/Meta-ethics

2.4.1 What is Meta-ethics?

Debating moral questions (e.g., famine relief obligation) raises two types of philosophical questions:

  • First order questions: Which party is right, and why? (Province of *normative ethics*).
  • Second order questions: What are the parties *doing* when debating? (Province of *metaethics*).

"In metaethics, we are concerned not with questions which are the province of normative ethics like 'Should I give to famine relief?'... but with questions about questions like these."

Meta-ethics tries to answer questions such as:

  • What does "good," "right," or "justice" mean?
  • What makes something good or right?
  • Is moral realism true?
  • Is morality irreducible, cognitive, or overriding?
  • Do intrinsic values exist?

Normative ethics seeks answers *and* insight into *why* the answer is right. Classic theories disagree on the 'why'. Examples:

  • Act-utilitarianism (give relief because *this action* contributes most to GHPGN).
  • Rule-utilitarianism (give relief because rule demanding it contributes most to GHPGN).
  • Kantianism (give relief because universal refusal generates inconsistency).

Meta-ethics Concerns

Normative ethics seeks general principles underlying moral practice, impacting practical problems. Meta-ethics concerned with questions about:

  1. Meaning: Semantic function of moral discourse? State facts or other role?
  2. Metaphysics: Do moral facts/properties exist? What are they like? Reducible to other facts or irreducible/*sui generis*?
  3. Epistemology/Justification: Moral knowledge possible? How know if judgments true/false? How justify claims?
  4. Phenomenology: How moral qualities represented in experience? Appear 'out there'?
  5. Moral psychology: Motivational state of moral judger? Connection between judgment and motivation?
  6. Objectivity: Moral judgments correct/incorrect? Can we find moral truth?

List not exhaustive, questions related. Positive answer to (f) seems to presuppose fact-stating function (a). List wider than 50 years ago when metaethics seen mainly as language study.

"[Metaethics] is not about what people ought to do. It is about what they are doing when they talk about what they ought to do." (Older view)

Ordinary language philosophy waned; metaethics now includes metaphysics, epistemology, phenomenology, psychology.

2.4.2 Cognitivism and Non-Cognitivism

Consider moral judgment: "Murder is wrong." What psychological state does it express?

Cognitivists: Moral judgments express beliefs. Beliefs can be true/false (are truth-apt). So, moral judgments capable of being true/false.

Non-cognitivists: Moral judgments express non-cognitive states (emotions, desires). These are not truth-apt. So, moral judgments not capable of being true/false. (Note: Desire *itself* isn't T/F, though "I have desire X" *can* be T/F).

2.4.2.1 Strong Cognitivism: Naturalism

Strong cognitivism: Moral judgments (a) are truth-apt, AND (b) can result from cognitively accessing facts making them true. Can be naturalist or non-naturalist.

Naturalism: Moral judgment true/false due to a *natural* state of affairs, accessed by true judgment. What is natural? G.E. Moore: Subject matter of natural sciences and psychology.

Natural property: Figures in natural science/psychology (e.g., property of conducing to GHPGN, preserving species). Natural state of affairs: Instantiation of natural property.

Naturalist cognitivists: Moral properties identical/reducible to natural properties.

  • Cornell realists (Sturgeon, Boyd, Brink): Moral properties are irreducible natural properties.
  • Naturalist reductionists: Moral properties reducible to other natural properties.
Both are moral realists: Moral facts/properties exist, independent of human opinion.

Strong Cognitivism Variations

2.4.2.1.1 Strong Cognitivism: Non-Naturalism

Non-naturalists: Moral properties not identical/reducible to natural properties. They are irreducible and *sui generis* (unique).
Two types:

  • Moore's ethical non-naturalism (*Principia Ethica*, 1903): Moral goodness is non-natural, simple, unanalysable property.
  • Contemporary version (McDowell, Wiggins, 1970s+).
Both are moral realists: Real moral facts/properties exist, independent of human opinion.

2.4.2.1.2 Strong Cognitivism without Moral Realism: Mackie's 'Error-Theory'

John Mackie: Moral judgments *are* truth-apt and, if true, would grant access to moral facts. However, moral judgments are *always false*.

Why? Simply no moral facts/properties of the required sort exist.

  • No plausible epistemology for accessing such facts/properties.
  • Such properties/facts would be "metaphysically queer" – unlike anything else. A moral property would have to motivate mere apprehension of it, which Mackie finds problematic.

Conclusion: No moral properties/facts. Positive moral judgments uniformly false. Our moral thinking involves radical error. Mackie is *not* a moral realist, but a moral *antirealist*.

Weak Cognitivism & Non-Cognitivism

2.4.2.1.3 Weak Cognitivism without Moral Realism: 'Best Opinion' Theories

Weak cognitivism: Moral judgments (a) are truth-apt, BUT (b) *cannot* be upshot of cognitive access to moral properties/states of affairs.

Agrees with strong cognitivism on (a), disagrees on (b). Example: Theory where our *best judgments* about morals *determine* extensions of moral terms, rather than tracking/detecting independent facts. (Extension = class of things predicate applies to).

Moral judgments T/F, but not based on tracking/accessing faculty. Rejects moral realism by denying facts are constitutively independent of human opinion (not by denying facts exist like error-theory).

2.4.3 Non-Cognitivism

Non-cognitivists: Deny moral judgments even apt to be true/false. Disagree with both weak/strong cognitivism.

Argument from moral psychology (example):

  • Cognitivist: Moral judgments express beliefs.
  • Motivation requires belief + desire (e.g., reach for fridge = belief water inside + desire water).
  • Internal/necessary fact: Sincerely judging X good motivates pursuing X.
  • So, if moral judgment expressed belief, it'd be belief *necessarily* connected to desire.
  • But, Hume: Beliefs/desires distinct existences; no necessary connection possible.
  • Therefore, moral judgments cannot express beliefs. Therefore, not truth-apt.

If judgments don't express beliefs, what do they express?

Non-Cognitivist Views & Frege-Geach Problem

Three versions of non-cognitivism:

  • J. Ayer's emotivism (1936): Moral judgments express emotions, sentiments of approval/disapproval.
  • Simon Blackburn's quasi-realism (1984): Moral judgments express dispositions to form sentiments of approval/disapproval.
  • Allan Gibbard's norm-expressivism (1990): Moral judgments express acceptance of norms.

Main challenge: Frege-Geach problem.
Emotivism: Judging "murder is wrong" = shouting "Boo for murder!" (evincing disapproval, not describing).
But what about conditional statements like "If murder is wrong, then murdering your mother-in-law is wrong"? This makes sense.
On emotivist interpretation, it *doesn't* make sense. (What would "If Boo for murder!, then Boo for murdering mother-in-law!" mean?) Difficulty embedding moral statements in complex sentences.

2.4.3.1 Internalism and Externalism, Humeanism and Anti-Humeanism

Premise in psychology argument: Internal/necessary connection between sincere moral judgment and motivation. This is internalism.

Responses & Meta-ethics Summary

Some cognitivists respond by denying internalism. Connection between judgment and motivation is only external/contingent. These are externalists.

Other cognitivists deny another premise: that motivation always involves *both* belief and desire (Humean theory of motivation). McDowell/Wiggins advance anti-Humean theory: beliefs *themselves* can be intrinsically motivating.

Generally, Meta-ethics:

  • Examines meaning of moral terms/concepts and relationships.
  • Explores origins of moral values (personhood, autonomy).
  • Considers difference between moral and other values.
  • Examines justification of moral claims.

Meta-ethics also poses questions like:

  • What does "life is sacred" mean?
  • Are moral claims personal view, religious belief, social standard, or objective?
  • If objective, what makes them so?
  • Link between human psychology and moral claims?

Chapter Summary

Ethics is crucial for students to adopt ethical behavior for a good life, promoting a healthy society and fulfilling individual experience. In globalization, "Ethics will be one of the enablers of competitiveness." Stressing ethics' centrality benefits humankind and society, fostering growth through ethical qualities.

Morality concerns habits, customs, ways of life assessed as good/bad, right/wrong. Moral values regulate behavior, maintain society, ensure peace/stability. Individuals have moral responsibility to respect societal values and avoid disturbing stability.

While utilitarianism, deontology, virtue theory are canonical, they evolve. Critiques lead to clarity and attunement to human living. Contemporary theories (principlism, narrative, feminist ethics) offer developments, fill gaps, acknowledge need to move from abstraction to concrete situations. This helps assess how moral theory guides decision-making and judgment.
